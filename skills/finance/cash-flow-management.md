# Cash Flow Management

This skill analyzes, forecasts, and optimizes business cash inflows and outflows to prevent liquidity crises and improve financial stability.

## Cash Flow Analysis Workflow

### 1. Assess Current Cash Position
- Identify current cash and cash equivalents on hand
- Review outstanding receivables (who owes money and when it's due)
- List all upcoming payables (bills, payroll, loans, taxes) with due dates
- Calculate the current cash runway: current cash ÷ monthly burn rate

### 2. Build a 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast
- Map all expected inflows by week: customer payments, loans, asset sales, investments
- Map all expected outflows by week: payroll, rent, suppliers, debt service, taxes
- Calculate weekly net cash position (inflows minus outflows)
- Identify weeks with projected negative balances (cash gaps)
- Flag any periods where balance drops below a 4-week operating expense buffer

### 3. Identify Cash Flow Problems
- **Slow receivables**: Average collection period > 45 days signals a problem
- **Accelerated payables**: Paying suppliers faster than collecting from customers
- **Seasonal gaps**: Revenue dips not matched by expense reductions
- **Overtrading**: Growing revenue faster than working capital can support
- **Debt concentration**: Large loan payments clustering in the same period

### 4. Apply Optimization Levers

**Accelerate Inflows:**
- Offer 2/10 net 30 early payment discounts to key customers
- Implement upfront deposits or milestone billing for projects
- Switch monthly invoicers to weekly or bi-weekly billing
- Follow up on receivables at 15 days (not 30)
- Explore invoice factoring or receivables financing for immediate cash

**Delay Outflows:**
- Negotiate extended payment terms with suppliers (net 60 or net 90)
- Use vendor credit cards to gain 30-day float
- Lease equipment instead of purchasing
- Defer non-critical capital expenditures
- Request payment plans for large tax liabilities

**Build Liquidity Reserves:**
- Establish a revolving line of credit before it's needed
- Target a minimum cash buffer equal to 8–12 weeks of operating expenses
- Move excess cash to high-yield savings or short-term instruments

### 5. Set Up Ongoing Monitoring
- Review actual vs. forecast cash position weekly
- Update the 13-week rolling forecast every Monday
- Track Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) and Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) monthly
- Alert triggers: cash balance falls below 6-week runway or any week projects negative

## Output Format

Produce a structured cash flow report with these sections:

**1. Current Cash Snapshot** (3–5 bullet points)
- Current cash on hand
- Total receivables due within 30/60/90 days
- Total payables due within 30/60/90 days
- Cash runway at current burn rate

**2. 13-Week Cash Flow Table**
| Week | Starting Balance | Inflows | Outflows | Net | Ending Balance |
|------|-----------------|---------|----------|-----|----------------|
Present 13 rows with specific dollar amounts or placeholders if data is missing.

**3. Risk Flags** (bulleted list)
- Identify each week with projected negative or critically low balance
- Name the specific cause (e.g., "Week 6: $42K payroll + $18K rent with only $31K projected inflows")

**4. Action Plan** (numbered priority list)
- List 3–7 specific actions ranked by impact and speed of implementation
- Each action includes: what to do, who owns it, and target completion date

**5. Key Metrics Summary**
- DSO (Days Sales Outstanding)
- DPO (Days Payable Outstanding)
- Cash Conversion Cycle
- Current Ratio
- Minimum projected cash balance and the week it occurs
