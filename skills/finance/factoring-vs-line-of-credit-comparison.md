# Factoring vs. Line of Credit Comparison

This skill produces a side-by-side true-cost and flexibility analysis of invoice factoring versus a revolving credit line, calibrated to the user's specific receivables volume, payment terms, and creditworthiness profile.

## Data Gathering

Before running the comparison, collect the following inputs (ask if not provided):

1. **Monthly invoice volume** — total dollar value of invoices issued per month
2. **Average days sales outstanding (DSO)** — how long customers typically take to pay (e.g., Net 30, Net 60, actual average)
3. **Factoring terms (if known)** — advance rate (e.g., 80–90%), factoring fee (e.g., 2–5% per invoice), recourse vs. non-recourse
4. **Credit line terms (if known)** — credit limit, interest rate (APR), draw fees, unused line fees
5. **Business credit profile** — approximate credit score range or years in business (affects line eligibility)
6. **Collections capability** — does the business want to outsource collections or retain control?

If the user does not have specific terms, use realistic market benchmarks: factoring advance rate 85%, fee 3% per 30-day cycle; credit line APR 10–14%, no draw fee.

## Analysis Steps

1. **Calculate effective APR for factoring**
   - Formula: (Factoring fee / Advance rate) × (365 / DSO) × 100
   - Example: (3% / 85%) × (365 / 45) = ~28.6% APR equivalent
   - Note any additional fees (wire fees, due diligence fees, monthly minimums)

2. **Calculate effective APR for the credit line**
   - Use stated APR plus any draw fees and unused line fees annualized
   - Compute interest cost on the same dollar amount held for the same DSO period

3. **Compute monthly cash cost for each option**
   - Apply each option's effective rate to the user's monthly invoice volume at their DSO
   - Show the dollar cost side by side for the same financing need

4. **Assess qualitative trade-offs across six dimensions:**
   - **Speed to cash** — factoring: 24–48 hours; credit line: draw anytime once established
   - **Qualification barrier** — factoring based on customer credit; LOC based on business credit
   - **Collections control** — factoring transfers or shares collections; LOC retains full control
   - **Scalability** — factoring scales with revenue automatically; LOC capped at approved limit
   - **Recourse risk** — recourse factoring creates liability if customer doesn't pay; LOC risk is on borrower only
   - **Relationship impact** — notification factoring alerts customers; confidential factoring and LOC do not

5. **Identify the dominant option** based on the user's profile:
   - Weak business credit + strong customer credit → lean factoring
   - Strong business credit + want control + lower cost → lean LOC
   - Rapid growth exceeding current LOC capacity → factoring advantage
   - Sensitive customer relationships → LOC or confidential factoring

6. **Flag any deal-breakers or red flags** (e.g., factoring fees exceeding gross margin, LOC covenants, concentration limits)
