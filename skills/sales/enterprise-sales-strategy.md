# Enterprise Sales Strategy

This skill helps sellers map stakeholders, build consensus, and move complex multi-threaded deals forward across long timelines with multiple decision-makers.

## Deal Navigation Framework

**1. Map the Buying Committee**
- Identify all stakeholders by role: Economic Buyer, Champion, Technical Evaluator, End User, Legal/Procurement blocker
- For each stakeholder, document: current sentiment (supporter/neutral/detractor), level of influence, and primary business concern
- Flag any missing relationships — unknown stakeholders are deal risks

**2. Diagnose Deal Health**
- Confirm a confirmed business problem with a quantified cost of inaction
- Verify there is an internal Champion with political capital who wants to see the deal close
- Check for a compelling event or hard deadline driving urgency
- If any of the three are missing, identify that as the primary deal risk to address first

**3. Build Multi-Thread Engagement**
- Never rely on a single point of contact — map at least two independent relationships
- Create stakeholder-specific value narratives: CFO cares about ROI and risk, CTO cares about integration and scalability, end users care about workflow pain relief
- Design a mutual action plan (MAP) with shared milestones and named owners on both sides

**4. Handle Procurement and Legal**
- Engage procurement early — ask "what does your typical vendor evaluation process look like?" in discovery
- Prepare a security/compliance one-pager proactively if the buyer is enterprise-grade
- Treat legal redlines as a project with a timeline; assign a contact and set a review deadline

**5. Unstick Stalled Deals**
- Diagnose stall cause: no urgency, lost Champion, budget frozen, competing priority, or hidden objection
- Re-engage with new insight, a relevant case study, or an executive-to-executive touchpoint
- Offer a limited proof-of-value (POV) or pilot with defined success criteria if the deal is stuck at evaluation

**6. Close and Negotiate**
- Present a single clean proposal — avoid options that create decision paralysis
- Anchor on value delivered, not product features, when justifying price
- Use a mutual close plan with a specific target date tied to their business outcome, not your quarter end

## Output Format

For each enterprise deal analyzed or strategy requested, produce:

**Deal Snapshot**
- Stakeholder map table: Name | Role | Sentiment | Key Concern | Relationship Owner
- Deal health scorecard: Business Problem (confirmed/unconfirmed), Champion (strong/weak/none), Compelling Event (exists/missing)

**Top 3 Deal Risks** — one sentence each, ranked by impact on close probability

**Next 3 Actions** — specific, assigned, time-bound (e.g., "Schedule CFO briefing with Champion by Thursday to present ROI model")

**Recommended Messaging** — one tailored value statement per key stakeholder segment (economic, technical, end user)

Keep the output scannable and under 500 words. Use tables where comparison aids clarity. Avoid generic sales advice — every recommendation must connect to the specific deal context provided.
