# Competitive Selling

This skill helps salespeople win competitive deals by reframing comparisons, neutralizing competitor strengths, and positioning their unique value so prospects choose them over alternatives.

## Competitive Selling Framework

### 1. Identify the Competitive Scenario
Determine which situation applies:
- **Direct comparison**: Prospect is actively evaluating a named competitor
- **Incumbent displacement**: Prospect already uses a competing product
- **Price objection**: Competitor is offering a lower price
- **Feature gap**: Competitor appears to have a feature you lack
- **Brand preference**: Prospect has a prior relationship or bias toward competitor

### 2. Acknowledge, Don't Attack
- Validate the competitor's strengths briefly and honestly — never dismiss them
- Attacking competitors makes you look defensive; acknowledging them builds trust
- Example: "Yes, [Competitor] is a solid option for teams that need X."

### 3. Reframe the Criteria
- Shift the conversation from where you're weak to where you win
- Identify the prospect's actual business outcomes (not just features they listed)
- Ask: "Beyond [the feature they mentioned], what does success look like in 6 months?"
- Reframe criteria around those outcomes: "The real question is which solution gets you to [outcome] faster."

### 4. Deliver Your Differentiating Wedge
Use this structure for each competitive response:
1. **Acknowledge** — Confirm what the competitor does well
2. **Pivot** — Introduce the dimension that matters more for this prospect
3. **Prove** — Back the claim with a specific data point, case study, or customer quote
4. **Question** — Ask a forward-looking question to lock in the reframe

Example wedge:
> "Competitor X is strong on reporting dashboards — no argument there. Where our customers see the difference is in time-to-value: our onboarding takes 2 weeks vs. their typical 6-8 weeks, which is why [Similar Company] was live and seeing ROI before their contract even renewed. How critical is fast deployment for your Q3 goals?"

### 5. Handle the Price Objection Specifically
- Never match price immediately — it signals low confidence
- Quantify the cost of the gap: "If our solution saves your team 5 hours/week, what's that worth annually?"
- Surface hidden costs in the competitor's offer: implementation fees, support tiers, renewal pricing
- Offer a risk-reduction alternative: pilot, phased rollout, or ROI guarantee instead of a discount

### 6. Address the Incumbent
- Acknowledge switching costs honestly: "Changing vendors takes real effort — I respect that."
- Quantify the cost of staying: what is the prospect losing or not gaining by staying put?
- Use a "future state" question: "If the switch were easy, would [our solution] be the obvious choice?"

### 7. Prepare Battlecard Talking Points
For any named competitor, structure your response around:
- **Their top 2 strengths** (what they genuinely do well)
- **Their top 2 weaknesses** (real gaps, not spin)
- **Your 2 winning differentiators** specific to this prospect's stated priorities
- **1 proof point** (customer, metric, or case study)

## Output Format

Produce a structured competitive response that includes:

**Scenario Summary** (1 sentence): Name the competitive situation and the prospect's apparent priority.

**Recommended Response Script** (3–6 sentences): A natural, conversational reply the salesperson can use verbatim or adapt. Must follow the Acknowledge → Pivot → Prove → Question structure.

**Differentiating Wedge** (bullet list): 2–3 specific points that shift evaluation criteria in your favor, each tied to a business outcome.

**Landmine Question** (1 question): A discovery question that exposes a known competitor weakness without naming it directly
