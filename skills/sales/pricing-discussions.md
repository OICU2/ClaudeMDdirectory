# Pricing Discussions

This skill helps Claude navigate pricing conversations by anchoring value, addressing cost objections with confidence, and closing deals at or near target price without reflexively offering discounts.

## Pricing Conversation Playbook

### Step 1: Diagnose the Objection Before Responding
Identify which type of pricing objection you're facing before crafting a response:
- **Sticker shock** — price feels high relative to expectation
- **Budget constraint** — genuine ceiling, not a negotiation tactic
- **Competitor comparison** — they have a lower quote in hand
- **Value doubt** — they don't believe the outcome is worth the cost
- **Negotiation tactic** — testing to see if you'll fold

Ask one clarifying question if the type is unclear: "Is it that the price is outside your budget entirely, or does it feel high for what's included?"

### Step 2: Anchor to Value Before Defending Price
Never defend the number alone — defend the outcome it buys:
- Restate the specific problem they came to solve
- Quantify the cost of NOT solving it (lost revenue, time, risk)
- Connect the price to the measurable result, not the deliverable
- Use the framing: "The investment is $X, and the outcome is Y — that's the value equation here."

### Step 3: Handle the Three Most Common Objections

**"Your competitor charges less"**
- Acknowledge without dismissing: "That's worth understanding."
- Ask what's included in the competitor quote — scope differences are almost always present
- Highlight one concrete differentiator that affects their specific outcome
- Do not match price; offer to adjust scope if budget is fixed

**"We don't have the budget right now"**
- Separate timing from price: "Is this a budget cycle issue or a ceiling on what this is worth?"
- Offer phased engagement or smaller starting scope — not a discount on full scope
- Create urgency around the cost of delay: "What does another quarter without this solved cost you?"

**"Can you sharpen the pencil / come down at all?"**
- Pause before responding — silence signals confidence
- Respond: "I want to make this work. Help me understand what's driving that ask — is it budget, or does the value not feel proportional?"
- If concession is warranted, trade scope for price, never give margin away for nothing

### Step 4: Close at Target Price
- Summarize the value exchanged, not the features delivered
- Give a clear next step with a deadline: "If we confirm by [date], we can start [specific milestone]"
- Avoid "let me know what you think" — it stalls; instead ask "Does this work for you, yes or no?"
- If they say no, ask what would need to change for it to be a yes — don't walk away without learning

### Step 5: Know When to Walk
- If they demand a discount with no scope change and no compelling reason, hold the line
- Say: "I respect your budget constraints, but I can't discount without adjusting what we deliver — that's how I protect the quality of the work."
- Walking away with confidence often reopens conversations; collapsing on price kills margin and trust

## Output Format

For each pricing conversation, produce:

1. **Objection Type** — one-line diagnosis of what's actually happening
2. **Response Script** — 3–5 sentences of exact language to use, written in first person, ready to say aloud
3. **Follow-up Question** — one question to keep the conversation moving forward
4. **Fallback Position** — what to offer if price cannot hold (scope reduction, phasing, or walk-away language)

Keep responses concise and confident. No hedging language ("maybe," "sort of," "I think"). Write as if the seller has done this a hundred times and believes the price is fair.
