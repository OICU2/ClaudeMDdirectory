# Sales Psychology Skill

This skill applies proven psychological principles — reciprocity, scarcity, social proof, authority, commitment, and loss aversion — to analyze and improve sales conversations, copy, and strategy.

## Core Framework: Psychological Principles Applied to Sales

### Step 1: Diagnose the Sales Context
Identify what the user is working on:
- **Cold outreach** (email, LinkedIn, cold call)
- **Sales page / landing page copy**
- **Live sales conversation or objection handling**
- **Follow-up sequence**
- **Pricing presentation**

### Step 2: Map Relevant Psychological Levers

Apply the following principles based on context:

**Reciprocity** — Give value first. Lead with a free insight, audit, resource, or compliment before asking. Example: "Here's one thing I noticed about your funnel..." before pitching.

**Social Proof** — Use specific, credible proof. Replace "many companies use us" with "47 SaaS teams in Series A used this to cut churn by 18%." Names, numbers, and niches outperform generic testimonials.

**Scarcity & Urgency** — Must be real to be effective. Deadline-based offers, limited cohort sizes, or price increases work. Fake urgency destroys trust. Frame as opportunity cost: "Every week without this costs you X."

**Authority** — Establish credibility fast. Credentials, client logos, published work, or a specific insight that demonstrates deep expertise. Position before pitching.

**Commitment & Consistency** — Get small yeses before asking for the big one. Micro-commitments (replying to an email, answering a diagnostic question) increase conversion on larger asks.

**Loss Aversion** — People fear losing more than they desire gaining. Frame the cost of inaction: "Without solving X, you'll continue losing Y per month" before describing the gain.

**Liking** — Mirror the prospect's language, reference shared context, and show genuine curiosity about their situation. People buy from people they like.

**The Zeigarnik Effect** — Open loops compel action. End messages or calls with an unresolved question or incomplete thought to drive follow-through.

### Step 3: Identify What's Missing or Weak
Review the user's existing copy, script, or approach and flag:
- Which principles are absent
- Where trust gaps exist
- Where the ask comes too early
- Where generic language undermines credibility
- Whether objections are being handled with logic instead of emotion first

### Step 4: Rewrite or Reconstruct
Produce a revised version applying the identified principles. Show before/after when possible. Label which principle each change applies.

### Step 5: Objection Handling Protocol
When handling specific objections, use this sequence:
1. **Acknowledge** the objection without flinching ("That makes sense")
2. **Isolate** it ("Is that the only thing holding you back?")
3. **Reframe** using psychology (loss aversion, social proof, or commitment)
4. **Redirect** to desired outcome ("What would it mean for you if this worked?")

## Output Format

Produce a structured response with the following sections:

**Diagnosis** (2–4 bullet points)
- Identify what psychological principles are missing or misapplied
- Note where the sales moment breaks down

**Revised Version**
- Full rewrite of the copy, script segment, or objection response
- Clearly formatted (use line breaks for scripts, paragraphs for copy)
- Length matches the original input — don't pad or cut unnecessarily

**Principle Annotations**
- Inline or appended labels showing which principle each section activates
- Format: [SOCIAL PROOF], [LOSS AVERSION], [COMMITMENT], etc.

**Quick Wins** (optional, 2–3 bullets)
- Additional tactics the user can apply immediately without a full rewrite
