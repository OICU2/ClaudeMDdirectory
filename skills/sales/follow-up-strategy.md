# Follow-Up Strategy

This skill designs personalized, multi-touch follow-up sequences for sales scenarios that give prospects genuine reasons to respond while keeping momentum on stalled or slow-moving deals.

## Follow-Up Sequence Builder

### Step 1: Gather Context
Before building the sequence, collect:
- **Stage**: Where is the deal? (initial outreach, post-demo, post-proposal, ghosting recovery, closed-lost re-engagement)
- **Last interaction**: What was said? What was the prospect's reaction or objection?
- **Timeline**: Do they have a known decision date or urgency signal?
- **Relationship depth**: Cold contact, warm intro, or existing relationship?
- **Product/service**: What are you selling and what value does it deliver?

### Step 2: Apply Sequence Logic by Stage

**Post-Initial Outreach (No Response)**
- Day 1: Send original message
- Day 3: Add a new data point or relevant insight (not a "just checking in")
- Day 7: Share a case study or social proof specific to their industry
- Day 14: Offer a different entry point (demo, audit, free resource)
- Day 21: Breakup message — give them an easy out, restate value clearly

**Post-Demo or Meeting (No Decision)**
- Day 1: Send recap + clear next step request
- Day 3: Address the most likely unstated objection proactively
- Day 7: Share a relevant customer success story
- Day 14: Ask a discovery question that reopens the conversation
- Day 21: Re-anchor to their stated business pain
- Day 30: Final nudge with a low-commitment offer

**Ghosting Recovery (Went Silent Mid-Deal)**
- Touch 1: Acknowledge the silence without guilt-tripping; restate original value
- Touch 2: Share something new (product update, relevant news, new ROI data)
- Touch 3: Ask if priorities have shifted — make it easy to say no or pause
- Touch 4: Breakup message that opens the door for future re-engagement

### Step 3: Write Each Message Using the VRA Framework
- **V — Value**: Lead with something useful to them, not a status check
- **R — Relevance**: Tie it to their industry, company, or stated goal
- **A — Ask**: One clear, low-friction call to action per message

### Step 4: Anti-Pushiness Rules
- Never use "just following up" or "circling back" as openers
- Never send two messages in a row without a new piece of value
- Always give prospects an easy, face-saving way to pause or say no
- Space messages based on deal size: enterprise = more patience, SMB = tighter cadence
- Stop the sequence if they explicitly ask you to

## Output Format

Produce a **numbered follow-up sequence** with the following structure for each touch:

```
**Touch [#] — Day [X]**
Channel: [Email / LinkedIn / Phone]
Subject line (if email): [Subject]
Message:
[Full message text, ready to send or lightly personalize]
Goal: [What this touch is designed to achieve]
```

Then append a **Sequence Summary** that includes:
- Total number of touches and overall timeline
- The core value thread running through the sequence
- One sentence on when to abandon the sequence
- Optional: suggested A/B variant for Touch 2 or 3

Length: 4–6 touches per sequence unless a longer enterprise cycle is specified. Messages should be 3–7 sentences each — concise and skimmable.
