# Competitor Mention Handling Playbook

This skill generates a structured, rep-ready playbook for responding when a prospect voluntarily names a competitor during discovery or evaluation, covering acknowledgment, probing, differentiation, and closing moves.

## Playbook Construction Steps

### 1. Gather Context
Before building the playbook, collect:
- **Named competitor** (e.g., Salesforce, HubSpot, a niche tool)
- **Stage of conversation** (early discovery, mid-evaluation, late-stage decision)
- **How the competitor was mentioned** (currently using it, evaluating it, heard about it, team prefers it)
- **Your product's name and top 3 differentiators** (if not provided, flag that these are needed)
- **Known weaknesses of the named competitor** relevant to this prospect's likely use case

### 2. Build the Playbook in Six Sections

**Section A — Acknowledge Without Disparaging**
Write 2–3 sentence openers that validate the prospect's awareness of the competitor without attacking it. The tone must be confident and neutral, not defensive.
- Example pattern: "Yes, [Competitor] is a well-known option. A lot of teams we talk to have evaluated them. Can I ask what specifically drew you to consider them?"

**Section B — Diagnostic Probes**
Write 4–6 discovery questions designed to surface gaps, frustrations, or unmet needs related to the competitor. Questions must be open-ended and prospect-focused, not leading.
- Focus areas: implementation pain, pricing surprises, missing features, support quality, team adoption issues, integration friction.

**Section C — Differentiation Bridges**
For each differentiator provided, write a one-sentence bridge that connects the differentiator directly to a likely prospect pain point surfaced by the probes.
- Format: "If [pain point] is a concern, [your product] handles this by [specific capability]."
- Avoid superlatives. Use concrete mechanics, not adjectives.

**Section D — Competitive Landmines**
List 3–5 known weaknesses of the named competitor phrased as questions the rep can ask to let the prospect surface them independently.
- Example pattern: "How important is [feature/outcome] to your team? That's an area where teams sometimes find limitations with [Competitor]."

**Section E — Objection Responses**
Write responses to the 3 most common objections that arise when this competitor is named:
1. "We've already invested in [Competitor]" — switching cost objection
2. "[Competitor] is cheaper" — price objection
3. "Our team already knows [Competitor]" — familiarity/change resistance objection

Each response: 3–4 sentences max, ends with a question to re-engage the prospect.

**Section F — Positioning Close**
Write a 3–4 sentence summary statement the rep can use to reframe the conversation toward a next step (demo, pilot, stakeholder call) without pressuring. Must acknowledge the competitor respectfully and pivot to value.

### 3. Flag Missing Inputs
If the user has not provided differentiators or the competitor is obscure, explicitly state what additional information is needed before the full playbook can be completed. Do not fabricate competitive intelligence.

## Output Format

Produce a single document with clearly labeled sections A through F using bold headers. Each section contains:
- **Header** (bold, e.g., **A. Acknowledge Without Disparaging**)
- **Purpose line** (one sentence explaining when/why to use this section)
- **Ready-to-use scripts or questions** (bulleted, written in first-person rep voice)
- **Coaching note** (one line on tone or delivery, italicized)

Total length: 500–900 words depending on how
