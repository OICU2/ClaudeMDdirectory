# Sales Presentations Skill

This skill helps craft and structure sales presentations that connect prospect pain to your solution, build urgency, and drive a clear next step.

## Presentation Framework

### 1. Diagnose Before You Build
Gather these inputs before structuring anything:
- Who is the audience? (titles, roles, decision-making power)
- What stage is the deal? (first call, demo, final presentation, renewal)
- What pain or goal prompted this conversation?
- What objections or competitors are in play?
- What is the desired outcome of this presentation? (next meeting, signed contract, approval)

### 2. Structure the Narrative (Problem-Solution-Proof-CTA)

**Opening Hook (60 seconds)**
- Lead with the prospect's world, not your product
- State a specific problem, trend, or cost they recognize
- Example: "Most [role] we talk to are losing X hours per week to Y — and it's costing them Z."

**The Problem Slide**
- Name the pain in the prospect's own language
- Quantify the impact: time, money, risk, or missed opportunity
- Make them feel the cost of inaction

**The Solution Slide**
- Introduce your product as the direct answer to the stated problem
- Lead with outcomes, not features
- Keep it to 3 core capabilities maximum

**Proof Section**
- Use a customer story with a matching profile (same industry, role, or problem)
- Structure: Situation → Challenge → Solution → Result
- Include a specific metric or quote

**Differentiation**
- State 2–3 reasons you win — tied to this prospect's priorities
- Avoid generic claims ("easy to use," "best in class")
- Use a comparison frame only if they've mentioned competitors

**Pricing / ROI (if applicable)**
- Anchor value before stating price
- Show ROI calculation using their numbers when possible
- Offer a clear tier or package tied to their stated need

**Call to Action**
- State the exact next step: "I'd like to schedule a 30-minute technical review with your team by Friday"
- Give them a reason to move now (timeline, limited capacity, event trigger)
- Never end with "Let me know if you have questions"

### 3. Handle the Room
- Pause after the problem slide and ask: "Does that resonate with what you're seeing?"
- Invite objections early: "What concerns do you have so far?"
- If you lose the room, stop and ask: "Is this the right direction, or should we focus somewhere else?"

### 4. Executive Presentations
- Cut to business impact in the first 2 minutes
- Skip feature depth — speak to risk, growth, and competitive position
- Bring data, not opinions
- Prepare a one-page leave-behind summary

## Output Format

When building a presentation, produce:

1. **Slide-by-slide outline** — title, one-sentence message per slide, supporting points (bullets), and any data or visuals to include
2. **Speaker notes** — 3–5 sentences per key slide with exact talking points and transition language
3. **Objection prep sheet** — top 3–5 objections likely to arise, with a concise response to each
4. **Closing script** — word-for-word language for the final 2 minutes including the CTA
5. **Follow-up email draft** — sent within 24 hours, recapping key points and confirming the next step

Length scales with deal stage: discovery call deck = 8–12 slides; final executive presentation = 12–18 slides. Every output should be editable, not generic — include placeholders like `[COMPANY NAME]`, `[PAIN POINT]`, and `[METRIC]` where prospect-specific details are needed.
