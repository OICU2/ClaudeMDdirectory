# High-Ticket Selling

This skill guides Claude to apply consultative, value-based sales frameworks that help position premium offerings, handle price objections, and close high-value clients with confidence.

## High-Ticket Sales Framework

### 1. Qualify First — Protect the Offer
- Identify whether the prospect has the budget, authority, need, and urgency (BANT)
- Never pitch before qualifying — high-ticket sales are disqualification processes as much as qualification ones
- Ask: "Who else is involved in this decision?" and "What's the cost of NOT solving this problem?"

### 2. Lead with Discovery, Not Features
- Ask pain-amplifying questions: "What has this problem cost you in the last 12 months — in time, revenue, or opportunity?"
- Uncover emotional drivers alongside logical ones
- Map their desired outcome in their own words — use their language in the pitch

### 3. Anchor Value Before Price
- Quantify the transformation: "If this saves you 10 hours/week at your billing rate, that's $X/year"
- Use ROI framing: investment vs. cost
- Reference social proof from similar clients at similar price points
- Never reveal price until value has been established

### 4. Present the Offer as a Solution, Not a Product
- Frame everything around their specific problem uncovered in discovery
- Structure: Problem → Consequence → Solution → Transformation → Investment
- Use tiered options (Good / Better / Best) to anchor the premium tier in the middle or top

### 5. Handle Objections with the ACA Method
- **Acknowledge**: "That's a completely fair concern."
- **Clarify**: "Help me understand — is it the total investment, or the timing?"
- **Address**: Respond directly with proof, reframes, or alternatives
- Common objections and responses:
  - "It's too expensive" → Reframe to cost of inaction or ROI
  - "I need to think about it" → Uncover the real hesitation; create urgency with consequences
  - "I need to talk to my partner/team" → Offer to join the conversation or provide a decision-ready summary

### 6. Close with Confidence
- Use assumptive or invitation closes: "The next step is X — does Tuesday or Thursday work better?"
- Avoid weak closes like "So… what do you think?" — they invite stalling
- Silence after the close is a tool — do not fill it
- If declined, ask: "What would need to be true for this to be a yes?"

### 7. Post-Close: Reduce Buyer's Remorse
- Immediately affirm the decision: "You've made a great call — here's exactly what happens next"
- Provide a clear onboarding or next-steps summary
- Set expectations to build confidence and prevent cancellation

## Output Format

When helping with a high-ticket sales situation, Claude should produce:

- **Sales Script or Conversation Flow**: Structured dialogue with specific questions, transitions, and closes — labeled by phase (Discovery / Pitch / Objection / Close)
- **Value Proposition Statement**: 3–5 sentences using the prospect's pain points and desired outcomes
- **Objection Response Bank**: Bulleted list of likely objections with 1–2 sentence responses for each
- **Price Presentation Script**: A short paragraph anchoring value before revealing investment
- **Follow-Up Message** (if requested): A 100–150 word message that re-anchors value and invites a next step

All outputs should be direct, confident in tone, and written to be spoken or sent — not just theorized.
