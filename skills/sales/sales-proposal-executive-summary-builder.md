# Sales Proposal Executive Summary Builder

This skill drafts a tight, persuasive executive summary for a sales proposal that speaks directly to the primary decision-maker's stated priorities, making it immediately clear why the proposed solution matters to them.

## Workflow

1. **Gather decision-maker context** — Ask for the decision-maker's title, role, and any known priorities, pressures, or goals they have expressed (e.g., cost reduction, market expansion, compliance, operational efficiency).

2. **Identify the core problem** — Confirm the specific business problem or pain point the proposal addresses. If not provided, ask for it explicitly before proceeding.

3. **Clarify the proposed solution** — Collect a brief description of what is being sold or proposed, including key differentiators or unique capabilities relevant to this buyer.

4. **Extract desired outcomes** — Ask what measurable results the buyer should expect (e.g., 20% cost reduction, faster time-to-market, risk mitigation). Use numbers and timeframes when available.

5. **Identify any competitive or urgency angle** — Note whether there is a reason to act now (contract expiry, competitive threat, regulatory deadline) and weave it in subtly.

6. **Draft the executive summary** using the collected inputs. Structure it around the buyer's world, not the seller's product. Lead with their problem, follow with the solution, and close with the outcome.

7. **Review for decision-maker fit** — Check that the language matches the buyer's level (CEO vs. CFO vs. VP of Operations use different priorities) and strip any jargon that does not serve them.

## Output Format

Produce a complete executive summary section with the following structure:

**[Company Name] Executive Summary** *(header)*

- **Opening paragraph (2–3 sentences):** State the decision-maker's core challenge or strategic priority in their own terms. Demonstrate understanding of their business context.

- **Solution paragraph (2–3 sentences):** Introduce the proposed solution clearly and connect each key capability directly to a stated priority. Avoid feature lists — translate features into business outcomes.

- **Value & outcomes paragraph (2–3 sentences):** Quantify the expected impact where possible. Use specific metrics, timeframes, or benchmarks. If exact numbers are unavailable, use ranges or qualitative outcomes framed in business terms.

- **Closing sentence (1 sentence):** Create a soft call-to-action or forward momentum statement (e.g., "The following proposal outlines exactly how [Company] will help [Buyer] achieve [goal] by [timeframe].").

**Length:** 150–250 words total.
**Tone:** Professional, confident, buyer-centric. No superlatives, no filler phrases like "best-in-class" unless tied to evidence.
**Format:** Paragraph prose, not bullet points, suitable for direct inclusion in a formal proposal document.
