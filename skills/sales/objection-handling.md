# Objection Handling

This skill surfaces battle-tested rebuttal frameworks for common sales objections so Claude can help reps respond with empathy, logic, and a clear path forward.

## Objection Response Framework

**Step 1 — Identify the objection type**
Classify the objection into one of five categories:
- **Price** ("too expensive," "no budget," "can't justify the cost")
- **Timing** ("not now," "maybe next quarter," "call me in six months")
- **Competitor** ("we use X already," "why are you better than Y")
- **Authority** ("I need to check with my boss," "not my decision")
- **Need** ("we don't need this," "we're fine with our current process")

**Step 2 — Acknowledge before rebutting**
Never dismiss the objection. Open with a validating statement:
- "That's a fair concern, and it comes up often."
- "I completely understand — let me share how others in your position thought about it."
- "You're right to raise that."

**Step 3 — Apply the AIC rebuttal structure**
- **Acknowledge** — Validate the concern in one sentence.
- **Isolate** — Confirm this is the only blocker: "If we could resolve X, is there anything else that would prevent you from moving forward?"
- **Counter** — Deliver a specific, evidence-backed response using one of the frameworks below.

**Step 4 — Rebuttal frameworks by objection type**

*Price:*
- Reframe as ROI: "What's the cost of not solving this problem for another year?"
- Break it down: "That's roughly $X per day — less than a daily coffee per team member."
- Anchor to value: "Our customers average Y% improvement in Z, which typically pays back the investment in N weeks."

*Timing:*
- Create urgency without pressure: "What would need to change by next quarter for this to be a priority?"
- Surface the real objection: Timing is rarely the true blocker — ask what's driving the delay.
- Quantify delay cost: "Each month without this typically costs companies like yours approximately $X."

*Competitor:*
- Differentiate on a specific axis: performance, support, integration, pricing model, or roadmap.
- Ask a comparison question: "What do you value most in your current solution — and where do you wish it did better?"
- Avoid badmouthing; focus on fit: "They're a solid option. Here's where we tend to win and where they do."

*Authority:*
- Qualify the decision process: "Who else is typically involved in decisions like this?"
- Offer to help champion: "Would it be useful if I put together a one-page summary you could share internally?"
- Request a joint call: "Could we set up 20 minutes with your team so I can answer their questions directly?"

*Need:*
- Quantify the hidden pain: "Many teams feel that way until we map their current process — want to spend 10 minutes doing that together?"
- Use a reference story: "A company similar to yours said the same thing. Here's what they found after a 30-day trial."
- Accept and pivot: "That's completely fair. Would it be worth a brief check-in in 90 days to see if anything changes?"

**Step 5 — Close the micro-commitment**
Every objection response must end with a forward motion question:
- "Does that address your concern?"
- "What would the next step look like from your side?"
- "Can we schedule 20 minutes to dig into that together?"
