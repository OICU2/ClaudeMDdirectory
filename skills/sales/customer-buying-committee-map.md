# Customer Buying Committee Map

This skill builds a structured map of every person involved in a purchasing decision at a target account, assigning roles, priorities, and aligned value propositions to each stakeholder so sellers can pursue the right conversations with the right messages.

## Buying Committee Mapping Process

### Step 1: Gather Account Intelligence
Ask the user for the following before building the map:
- Company name and deal context (what are you selling, what stage is the deal?)
- Names, titles, and departments of known contacts
- Any stated priorities, concerns, or objections already surfaced
- Unknown roles the user suspects exist but hasn't identified yet

If information is incomplete, flag gaps explicitly and note them as "Unknown — needs discovery."

### Step 2: Assign Roles to Each Stakeholder
Classify every identified person into one of these roles:
- **Economic Buyer** — Final budget authority; signs the contract
- **Champion** — Internal advocate who wants the deal to succeed
- **Technical Evaluator** — Assesses product fit, security, integrations
- **End User** — Directly uses the product; cares about usability
- **Legal/Compliance** — Reviews contracts, data privacy, risk
- **Blocker** — Actively resistant or has competing interests
- **Influencer** — Shapes opinion without formal authority

### Step 3: Profile Each Stakeholder
For each person, document:
1. **Role in Committee** (from Step 2)
2. **Business Priority** — What does this person care most about? (cost reduction, risk mitigation, team productivity, career visibility, etc.)
3. **Likely Objection** — What concern or barrier might this person raise?
4. **Engagement Status** — Engaged / Neutral / Unengaged / Hostile
5. **Mapped Value Proposition** — One specific message that connects the product to their priority
6. **Recommended Next Action** — One concrete step to advance or deepen this relationship

### Step 4: Identify Coverage Gaps and Risks
After profiling all known stakeholders:
- Flag any critical roles with no identified contact (e.g., "No Economic Buyer identified")
- Highlight stakeholders marked Hostile or Unengaged who hold significant influence
- Note if the Champion has sufficient access to the Economic Buyer
- Call out any single points of failure (deal depends too heavily on one person)

### Step 5: Recommend Engagement Strategy
Summarize a prioritized action plan:
- Who to engage first and why
- Which relationships need immediate attention or risk mitigation
- What information still needs to be discovered and from whom

## Output Format

Produce the following structure:

---

**Account:** [Company Name]
**Deal:** [Product/Solution being sold]
**Stage:** [Current deal stage]
**Last Updated:** [Today's date]

---

### Stakeholder Map

| Name | Title | Role | Engagement | Priority | Key Objection | Value Prop | Next Action |
|------|-------|------|------------|----------|---------------|------------|-------------|
| [Name] | [Title] | [Role] | [Status] | [Top priority] | [Likely objection] | [Tailored message] | [Specific action] |

*(Repeat one row per stakeholder. Flag unknowns as "TBD — needs discovery.")*

---

### Coverage Gaps & Risks
- [Bullet list of missing roles, hostile stakeholders, or structural risks]

---

### Recommended Engagement Priorities
1. [Highest priority action — who, what, why]
2. [Second priority]
3. [Third priority]

---

### Open Discovery Questions
- [List questions the seller needs to answer to complete the map]

---

Keep the map scannable and action-oriented. Every cell in the table must contain a specific insight, never generic placeholders. Flag any field where real information is missing
