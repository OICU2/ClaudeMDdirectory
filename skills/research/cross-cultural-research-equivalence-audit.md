# Cross-Cultural Research Equivalence Audit

This skill audits survey or interview instruments for cultural equivalence issues—flagging translation risks, culturally embedded assumptions, and response scale incompatibilities—and delivers actionable revision recommendations for each flagged item.

## Audit Workflow

### 1. Intake and Scope Clarification
- Identify the target cultural/linguistic contexts (e.g., countries, language groups, subpopulations).
- Note the construct(s) being measured and the intended respondent populations.
- If target contexts are not specified, flag this as a prerequisite and ask before proceeding.

### 2. Item-Level Screening

For each survey item or interview question, evaluate across five risk dimensions:

**a. Translation Risk**
- Flag idioms, slang, colloquialisms, or metaphors with no direct equivalents (e.g., "feeling blue," "burning bridges").
- Flag double-barreled questions that become ambiguous when translated.
- Flag negated items that behave inconsistently across languages with different negation syntax.

**b. Culturally Specific Assumptions**
- Flag items assuming universally shared institutions (e.g., "your therapist," "your HR department," "your mortgage").
- Flag items referencing culture-bound behaviors, life events, or social structures (e.g., dating norms, family configurations, civic participation forms).
- Flag items where the construct itself may be culture-specific or carry different valence (e.g., "individualism," "privacy," "assertiveness").

**c. Response Scale Incompatibility**
- Flag Likert or numeric scales where endpoint meaning may differ (e.g., acquiescence bias in collectivist cultures, extreme response style variation).
- Flag scales with labeled midpoints that translate ambiguously.
- Flag forced-choice formats that conflict with cultural norms around directness or disagreement.

**d. Conceptual/Construct Equivalence**
- Flag items where the underlying concept may not exist, be labeled differently, or carry different meaning across target cultures.
- Note constructs requiring back-translation validation or cognitive interviewing.

**e. Formal/Functional Equivalence**
- Flag items where the behavioral referent differs across cultures (e.g., "exercise regularly," "eat a healthy diet," time-use questions).
- Flag items measuring equivalence of stimuli that may not be stimulus-equivalent cross-culturally (e.g., income brackets, education levels).

### 3. Instrument-Level Assessment
- Evaluate overall reading level and whether it transfers across education systems.
- Assess section ordering for cultural appropriateness (e.g., demographic questions first vs. last).
- Note whether instructions assume familiarity with survey-taking conventions that may vary.

### 4. Prioritization
- Assign each flagged item a risk level: **High** (likely to produce non-equivalent data), **Medium** (requires revision or cognitive testing), **Low** (minor adaptation recommended).

## Output Format

Produce a structured audit report with the following sections:

**INSTRUMENT EQUIVALENCE AUDIT REPORT**

**Target Contexts:** [List cultural/linguistic populations]
**Total Items Reviewed:** [N]
**Items Flagged:** [N] | High Risk: [N] | Medium Risk: [N] | Low Risk: [N]
