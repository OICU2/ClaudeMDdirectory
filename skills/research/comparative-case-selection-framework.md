# Comparative Case Selection Framework

This skill guides researchers through principled selection and justification of cases for comparative studies, applying Mill's methods and systematic case selection logic to match research questions with appropriate comparison strategies.

## Case Selection Workflow

### Step 1: Clarify the Research Goal
Identify what the comparison is meant to explain:
- Causal mechanism (why does X produce Y?)
- Scope conditions (under what conditions does X lead to Y?)
- Outcome variation (why do cases differ on Y?)
- Theory testing (does X theory hold across contexts?)

### Step 2: Identify the Outcome of Interest
Pin down the dependent variable:
- Is it binary (presence/absence) or continuous (degree)?
- Is there variation across potential cases, or is it constant?
- Avoid selecting only on the dependent variable — ensure both positive and negative cases are considered.

### Step 3: Choose a Case Selection Strategy

**Most-Similar Systems Design (MSSD)**
- Cases share as many background conditions as possible
- Cases differ on the key independent variable and the outcome
- Logic: Hold context constant, isolate the causal factor
- Best for: Ruling out rival explanations, testing specific mechanisms
- Example: Comparing two Nordic countries with similar institutions but different electoral rules

**Most-Different Systems Design (MDSD)**
- Cases differ across background conditions
- Cases share the key independent variable and the outcome
- Logic: If the same cause produces the same outcome across diverse contexts, the relationship is robust
- Best for: Establishing generalizability, identifying universal causes
- Example: Comparing a European and a Latin American country that both experienced democratic backsliding via different historical paths

**Diverse Cases**
- Deliberately select cases that span the full range of the outcome variable
- Best for: Descriptive work, establishing what variation looks like, theory building

**Typical Cases**
- Select cases that are representative of a broader pattern
- Best for: Illustrating mechanisms, process tracing within a confirmed relationship

**Deviant/Outlier Cases**
- Cases that do not fit the expected pattern
- Best for: Refining theory, identifying omitted variables

**Pathway Cases**
- Cases where the causal mechanism is expected to be clearly visible
- Best for: Process tracing, qualitative deep dives

### Step 4: Apply Practical Filters
Screen candidate cases against:
- **Data availability**: Are the required variables measurable for each case?
- **Temporal scope**: Do cases fall within the relevant time period?
- **Conceptual equivalence**: Does the key concept mean the same thing across cases?
- **Researcher access**: Are archives, interviews, or records accessible?
- **N constraints**: How many cases can the study realistically support?

### Step 5: Document Selection Criteria Explicitly
For each selected case, record:
- Why it was included (which criterion it satisfies)
- How it scores on the key independent variable
- How it scores on the outcome variable
- What rival explanations it helps rule in or out
- Any known weaknesses or boundary conditions

### Step 6: Flag Selection Biases
Actively check for:
- Selection on the dependent variable (all cases have the same outcome)
- Availability bias (using familiar or accessible cases without justification)
- Confirmation bias (selecting cases known to support the hypothesis)
- Galton's problem (cases that are not independent due to diffusion or shared history)

## Output Format

Produce a structured **Case Selection Report** with the following sections:

**1. Research Question and Outcome Variable**
One paragraph stating what is being explained and how it is operationalized.

**2. Selection Strategy**
Name the design logic chosen (MSSD, MDSD, diverse, typical, deviant, pathway) and explain in 2–3 sentences why it fits the research question.

**3. Case Roster Table**
A markdown table with columns:
| Case | Key IV Score | Outcome Score | Selection Rationale | Data Availability | Known
