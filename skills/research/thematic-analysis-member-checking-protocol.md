# Thematic Analysis Member-Checking Protocol

This skill generates a complete, structured member-checking protocol that guides researchers through returning derived themes to participants or experts for verification, refinement, and validation of qualitative findings.

## Member-Checking Protocol Design

### Phase 1: Pre-Check Preparation

1. **Summarize themes for lay audiences** — Strip jargon from theme labels and write a 2–3 sentence plain-language description of each theme, including supporting quotes or paraphrased examples drawn directly from participant data.
2. **Select checker pool** — Identify whether checkers should be:
   - Original participants (for resonance and accuracy)
   - Subject-matter experts (for domain completeness)
   - Both (for triangulated validation)
3. **Define scope clearly** — State explicitly what participants are being asked to evaluate: theme labels, theme descriptions, supporting evidence, relationships between themes, or missing themes.
4. **Set conflict-of-interest safeguards** — Note if any participants may have social desirability bias toward certain themes; plan for anonymous response options if needed.

### Phase 2: Instrument Construction

5. **Build a structured review form** with these sections:
   - **Recognition check**: "Does this theme reflect something from your experience? (Yes / Partially / No)"
   - **Accuracy rating**: 1–5 scale on how accurately the description captures the idea
   - **Completeness probe**: open field — "Is anything missing or misrepresented?"
   - **Label feedback**: "Does the theme title feel accurate or appropriate to you?"
   - **Rank or prioritize** (optional): ask checkers to rank themes by importance or salience
6. **Include an "emergent themes" prompt** — Ask: "Is there a pattern or idea important to you that you don't see represented here?"
7. **Cap cognitive load** — Limit review to no more than 7 themes per session; split larger theme sets across multiple rounds.

### Phase 3: Delivery and Administration

8. **Choose delivery method** based on study context:
   - Asynchronous (email/survey): suitable for sensitive topics or geographically dispersed participants
   - Synchronous (interview/focus group): richer dialogue, better for complex or contested themes
   - Hybrid: distribute materials in advance, then discuss
9. **Provide a worked example** — Show participants one completed review of a neutral/dummy theme so they understand the task before evaluating real findings.
10. **Set a response window** — Recommend 5–10 business days for async formats; schedule sync sessions within 2–4 weeks of initial analysis completion.

### Phase 4: Analyzing Checker Feedback

11. **Tally quantitative responses** — Calculate agreement rates per theme; flag any theme with less than 60% recognition or accuracy scores below 3/5 for revision.
12. **Code qualitative comments** — Apply a second-pass open coding to free-text responses; look for patterns in what checkers say is missing, inaccurate, or overstated.
13. **Classify disagreements** by type:
   - Factual correction (requires theme revision)
   - Emphasis disagreement (adjust weighting or description)
   - Perspective gap (may warrant a new sub-theme or theme split)
   - Idiosyncratic outlier (document but do not automatically revise)
14. **Document all changes with rationale** — Maintain an audit trail showing original theme, checker feedback received, decision made, and final revised theme.

### Phase 5: Reporting

15. **Write a member-checking summary section** for the methods portion of the final report, including:
   - Who participated (n, role, recruitment method)
   - What was shared and how
   - Response rate and timeline
   - Summary of feedback received
   - Changes made and justification for changes
