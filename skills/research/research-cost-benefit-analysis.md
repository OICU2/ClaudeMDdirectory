# Research Cost-Benefit Analysis

This skill performs a structured evaluation of whether a proposed research initiative justifies its resource investment by weighing estimated costs against the decision-making value of expected findings.

## Analysis Workflow

1. **Clarify the research objective**: Identify the specific question the research aims to answer and the decision it is intended to inform. If unclear, ask before proceeding.

2. **Estimate costs**: Break down resource requirements across four dimensions:
   - *Time*: Hours of researcher, analyst, and stakeholder time; calendar duration
   - *Financial*: Direct spend (tools, participants, vendors, subscriptions, travel)
   - *Opportunity cost*: What else could those resources accomplish
   - *Organizational burden*: Coordination overhead, approvals, distraction from other priorities

3. **Assess expected value**: Score the research across five factors:
   - *Decision impact*: How significantly will findings change the decision or course of action (scale 1–5)
   - *Decision urgency*: How time-sensitive is the decision the research informs (scale 1–5)
   - *Confidence gap*: How much uncertainty currently exists that research would reduce (scale 1–5)
   - *Reversibility*: How costly is it to make the wrong decision without this research (scale 1–5)
   - *Uniqueness*: Is this information already available or easily approximated (scale 1–5)

4. **Check for blockers**: Flag any conditions that would invalidate the research regardless of cost — e.g., findings that cannot realistically change the decision, research that will arrive too late, or data that cannot be ethically or practically obtained.

5. **Calculate net value**: Combine cost estimates and value scores into a plain-language verdict. Do not use a false-precision formula; render a reasoned judgment.

6. **Generate a recommendation**: Produce one of four outcomes:
   - **Proceed** — investment is clearly justified
   - **Proceed with scope reduction** — valuable but over-engineered; suggest trimming
   - **Explore cheaper alternatives** — existing data, expert interviews, or desk research may suffice
   - **Do not proceed** — cost exceeds plausible value; explain why

## Output Format

Produce a structured report with the following sections, using headers:

**Research Proposal Summary** (2–3 sentences restating the initiative and its purpose)

**Cost Estimate**
- Table or bullet list with Time, Financial, Opportunity Cost, and Organizational Burden rows
- Include a rough total effort figure (e.g., "~40 person-hours over 3 weeks, ~$8,000 direct cost")

**Value Assessment**
- One sentence per factor (Decision Impact, Urgency, Confidence Gap, Reversibility, Uniqueness)
- Score each 1–5 with a one-line rationale

**Blockers or Risks** (bullet list; omit section if none)

**Recommendation**
- Bold label (Proceed / Proceed with scope reduction / Explore cheaper alternatives / Do not proceed)
- 3–5 sentences explaining the reasoning
- If applicable, one concrete alternative or modification to consider

Total length: 300–600 words. Use plain language. Avoid academic hedging.
