# Data Collection Instrument Comparison

This skill systematically evaluates and compares research data collection instruments against a stated research objective, producing a structured trade-off analysis that helps researchers select the most appropriate method.

## Evaluation Workflow

1. **Clarify the research objective** — If not provided, ask: What is the research question? What population is being studied? What is the timeline and budget? Is the goal exploratory, descriptive, or causal?

2. **Identify candidate instruments** — Based on the objective, select the most relevant instruments to compare from: structured surveys, semi-structured interviews, focus groups, ethnographic observation, experiments, secondary data/archival analysis, diary studies, and sensor/behavioral data.

3. **Evaluate each instrument across six dimensions:**
   - **Data type produced** (numeric/quantitative vs. narrative/qualitative)
   - **Sample size feasibility** (how many participants can realistically be reached)
   - **Cost and resource requirements** (time, money, expertise needed)
   - **Validity risks** (social desirability bias, recall bias, observer effect, etc.)
   - **Depth vs. breadth trade-off** (richness of insight vs. generalizability)
   - **Fit to research question** (whether the instrument can actually answer the question)

4. **Identify constraints** — Flag any mismatch between instrument requirements and stated constraints (e.g., a survey requiring 500 responses when only 20 participants are accessible).

5. **Make a ranked recommendation** — Recommend a primary instrument and, where appropriate, a mixed-methods pairing. Justify the recommendation with direct reference to the research objective and constraints.

## Output Format

Produce a structured comparison with the following sections:

**Research Objective Summary** (1–2 sentences restating the goal)

**Instrument Comparison Table**
| Instrument | Data Type | Sample Scale | Cost | Key Validity Risk | Fit to Objective |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
*(Include 3–5 instruments)*

**Trade-Off Analysis** (3–5 bullet points highlighting the most important tensions, e.g., depth vs. generalizability, cost vs. richness)

**Recommendation**
- Primary instrument with 2–3 sentence justification
- Optional secondary instrument if mixed methods adds value
- One concrete implementation note (e.g., "Use a 5-point Likert scale to enable parametric analysis" or "Limit interview sample to 12–15 for thematic saturation")

**Risks to Monitor** (2–3 bullets on validity or feasibility risks to watch during execution)

Total length: 400–700 words. Use plain language; avoid jargon unless the researcher has used it first.
