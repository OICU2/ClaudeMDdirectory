# Field Research Skill

This skill guides Claude through designing and executing systematic observational research that captures authentic human behavior, environmental context, and real-world patterns that surveys and interviews miss.

## Field Research Workflow

### 1. Define the Research Question
- State a single, focused observation goal (e.g., "How do commuters navigate the transfer station during peak hours?")
- Identify the subject population and target environment
- Clarify what counts as a meaningful observation versus background noise

### 2. Design the Observation Protocol
- Choose observation stance: complete observer, observer-as-participant, or participant-as-observer
- Set session duration (minimum 2 sessions recommended), timing, and location criteria
- Define specific behaviors, interactions, or artifacts to record
- Create a field note template with these fields: timestamp, setting description, subject actions, verbatim quotes (if any), researcher reactions, emerging questions

### 3. Conduct Observations
- Arrive early to establish baseline environment conditions
- Record continuously using thick description — sensory details, spatial layout, who is present
- Log critical incidents: moments of friction, surprise, workaround, or emotional response
- Note what does NOT happen (absent expected behaviors are data)
- Debrief within 1 hour of each session to capture fading memory

### 4. Analyze Field Notes
- Code notes by themes: behaviors, pain points, environmental factors, social dynamics
- Identify recurring patterns across sessions (minimum 3 occurrences = pattern)
- Map contradictions between observed behavior and stated/assumed behavior
- Generate "How Might We" questions from each major friction point

### 5. Synthesize Findings
- Cluster patterns into 3–5 insight statements using the format: "[Subject] does [behavior] because [underlying need/constraint], which means [implication]"
- Identify one surprising finding that challenges prior assumptions
- Flag gaps requiring follow-up observation or triangulation with other methods

## Output Format

Produce a **Field Research Report** with these sections:

**Research Question** — 1 sentence stating what was studied and why

**Context** — 2–3 sentences describing the environment, subjects, and observation conditions (dates, duration, location type)

**Key Observations** — Bulleted list of 5–10 specific, concrete observations with timestamps or session references; no interpretation yet

**Patterns Identified** — 3–5 named patterns, each with: pattern name, supporting evidence (minimum 2 observations), and frequency

**Insight Statements** — 3–5 statements in the "[Subject] does X because Y, which means Z" format

**Surprises & Contradictions** — 1–3 findings that challenge assumptions, stated plainly

**Recommended Next Steps** — 2–4 specific follow-up actions (additional sessions, interviews to schedule, hypotheses to test)

Length: 400–800 words for a single-site study; scale up proportionally for multi-site. Use plain prose for context sections; use bullets for observations and patterns. Avoid jargon unless defined on first use.
