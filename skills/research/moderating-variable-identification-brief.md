# Moderating Variable Identification

Analyzes a provided research design or hypothesis and surfaces candidate moderating variables that could explain when, for whom, or under what conditions the focal relationship holds — used whenever a researcher needs to identify boundary conditions or account for conditional effects.

## Identification Workflow

1. **Extract the core relationship**: Identify the independent variable (IV), dependent variable (DV), and any stated mediators from the provided research design.
2. **Map the theoretical mechanism**: Determine *why* the IV is expected to affect the DV — this mechanism guides which moderators are plausible.
3. **Scan across moderator categories** — for each, generate specific candidates relevant to the study:
   - **Individual-level**: personality traits, demographics, prior experience, cognitive style, motivation
   - **Contextual/situational**: time pressure, resource availability, social presence, task complexity
   - **Organizational/structural**: culture, policies, team composition, industry sector
   - **Relational**: trust, familiarity, power dynamics between parties
   - **Temporal**: stage of a process, duration of exposure, measurement timing
4. **Apply the "changes the slope" test**: For each candidate, ask — would high vs. low levels of this variable plausibly strengthen, weaken, or reverse the IV→DV relationship? Discard candidates that only shift the intercept.
5. **Prioritize by feasibility**: Flag which moderators are measurable with standard instruments vs. require custom scales or hard-to-obtain data.
6. **State the interaction hypothesis**: For each retained moderator, write one sentence in standard moderation form: "The effect of [IV] on [DV] will be stronger/weaker when [moderator] is high vs. low."

## Output Format

Produce a structured brief with the following sections:

**Focal Relationship Summary** (1–2 sentences restating the IV→DV link as understood)

**Candidate Moderators** — a numbered list of 5–8 variables, each entry containing:
- Variable name and brief definition (1 sentence)
- Rationale for moderation (1–2 sentences on the mechanism)
- Direction of the expected interaction (strengthens / weakens / may reverse)
- Measurability note (existing scale available / custom measure needed / archival proxy possible)
- Interaction hypothesis in plain language (1 sentence)

**Top 3 Recommendations** — ranked by theoretical strength and practical feasibility, with a one-sentence justification for each ranking.

**Gaps to Clarify** — 2–3 questions to ask the researcher if the design was underspecified (e.g., sample population, theoretical framework used, available data sources).

Keep the full brief under 600 words. Use plain academic language; avoid jargon without definition.
