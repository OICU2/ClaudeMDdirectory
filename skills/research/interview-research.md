# Interview Research Skill

This skill designs rigorous qualitative interview frameworks, generates question guides, and synthesizes interview transcripts into structured insights that reveal what people actually think, feel, and do.

## Interview Research Workflow

### 1. Define the Research Objective
Before generating any questions, clarify:
- **Who** is being interviewed (role, context, relationship to topic)
- **What** decision or question the research must answer
- **What** you already know vs. what you need to learn
- **How many** interviews are planned (affects depth vs. breadth tradeoff)

### 2. Build the Interview Guide

Structure every guide in four phases:

**Warm-up (5 min)**
- Open with context-free, easy questions about the person's role or daily routine
- Never mention the core topic yet — establish rapport and baseline behavior
- Example: "Walk me through what a typical Tuesday looks like for you."

**Current State Exploration (15–20 min)**
- Ask about actual past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior
- Use the "last time" technique: "Tell me about the last time you had to [do X]."
- Probe for process, workarounds, frustrations, and what they wish existed
- Avoid leading questions — never embed the answer in the question

**Deep Dive (15–20 min)**
- Pursue the 3–5 most important unknowns from your research objective
- Use the ladder technique: ask "why" or "what matters about that" at least twice per answer
- Silence is a tool — pause 3–5 seconds after answers before speaking

**Wrap-up (5 min)**
- Ask: "Is there anything I didn't ask that you think I should know?"
- Ask: "Who else should I be talking to about this?"

### 3. Question Writing Rules
- Write questions at a 6th-grade reading level
- One question at a time — never bundle two questions
- Avoid yes/no questions; every question should require a narrative answer
- Banned words in questions: "would," "could you imagine," "do you think," "usually"
- Use instead: "tell me about," "walk me through," "describe," "what happened when"

### 4. Synthesize Transcripts into Insights
When given raw transcripts or notes, produce structured analysis using this method:

**Extract raw observations first**
- Pull direct quotes and specific behaviors — no interpretation yet

**Identify patterns**
- Group observations that point to the same underlying truth
- Note contradictions between what people say and what they do

**Generate insights**
- An insight = observation + so-what implication
- Format: "[Specific behavior/belief observed] which means [implication for the research question]"

**Surface surprises**
- Flag anything that contradicts your prior assumptions
- These are the most valuable findings

## Output Format

### For Interview Guide Requests
Produce a formatted document with:
- **Research objective** (1–2 sentences)
- **Participant screener criteria** (3–5 bullet points)
- **Full interview guide** with all four phases, time estimates per section, and 3–5 questions per section
- **Probing follow-up bank** — 8–10 universal probes to use anywhere (e.g., "What made that hard?", "Can you say more about that?")
- **Logistics checklist** — recording consent, note-taking setup, incentive confirmation

### For Transcript Synthesis Requests
Produce a structured findings document with:
- **Top 3–5 insights** (each with supporting quotes from at least 2 participants)
- **Mental model summary** — how interviewees understand the problem space
- **Key tensions and contradictions** found across participants
- **Behavior vs. stated preference gaps** (what they do vs. what they say)
- **Open questions** — what these interviews raise that still needs answering
- **Recommended next steps
