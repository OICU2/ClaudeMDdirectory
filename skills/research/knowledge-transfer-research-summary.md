# Knowledge Transfer Research Summary

This skill converts completed research outputs into structured, decision-ready briefs that strip technical complexity and surface only what operational or policy audiences need to act.

## Workflow

1. **Identify the research outputs**: Ask the user to provide or paste the research material — paper, report, dataset summary, literature review, or findings document. If not provided, ask before proceeding.

2. **Identify the target audience**: Determine who will receive the summary (e.g., executive team, policy board, operations manager, funding body). If unclear, ask one focused question: "Who is the primary decision-maker this summary is for?"

3. **Extract the core findings**: Pull out 3–5 specific, evidence-backed findings. Discard methodology detail, statistical nuance, and academic framing unless directly relevant to a decision.

4. **Map findings to decisions**: For each finding, identify what decision, action, or policy change it supports or challenges. If a finding has no actionable implication, flag it as background context only.

5. **Identify constraints and caveats**: Note only the limitations that materially affect how findings should be applied. Do not list academic caveats that do not change the recommendation.

6. **Draft recommendations**: Write 2–5 concrete recommendations using action verbs. Each recommendation must be directly traceable to at least one finding. Frame in terms of what the audience should do, approve, fund, halt, or investigate.

7. **Assign confidence levels**: Label each recommendation as High / Medium / Low confidence based on evidence strength, sample size, replicability, or recency of the research.

8. **Review for jargon**: Scan the full output and replace technical or discipline-specific terms with plain language equivalents. If a term must be kept, define it in one sentence inline.

## Output Format

Produce a structured brief with the following sections in order:

---

**Research Summary Brief**

**Source Material**: [Title or description of the research being summarized]
**Prepared For**: [Audience role or team]
**Date**: [Today's date]

---

**Bottom Line Up Front**
One to two sentences stating the single most important takeaway and its immediate implication for the audience.

---

**Key Findings**
Numbered list of 3–5 findings. Each finding:
- One sentence stating what the research found
- One sentence stating why it matters to this audience

---

**Recommendations**
Numbered list of 2–5 recommendations. Each recommendation:
- Action statement (begins with a verb: Implement, Discontinue, Investigate, Allocate, Revise, etc.)
- Supporting finding reference (e.g., "Based on Finding 2")
- Confidence level: **High / Medium / Low**
- One sentence on what inaction risks

---

**Important Limitations**
Bullet list of 1–3 caveats that materially affect how recommendations should be applied. No others.

---

**Suggested Next Steps**
Two to three concrete next steps with suggested owners or roles where possible.

---

Total length: 400–700 words. Use plain language throughout. No abstract, no literature review framing, no passive voice.
