# Research Question to Methodology Mapper

This skill analyzes a research question and systematically recommends the most appropriate research methodology, explaining the reasoning and trade-offs so researchers can make defensible design decisions.

## Methodology Mapping Workflow

**Step 1: Extract and Clarify the Research Question**
- Identify the core phenomenon or relationship being studied
- Classify the question type: exploratory (what/how/why), descriptive (how much/how many), explanatory (what causes), predictive, or evaluative
- If the question is vague, ask the researcher to state: what they want to know, about whom or what, and in what context

**Step 2: Assess the Ontological and Epistemological Position**
- Determine if the researcher treats reality as objective and measurable (positivist) or socially constructed and context-dependent (interpretivist)
- Ask directly if needed: "Are you looking for generalizable patterns or deep contextual understanding?"
- Note: mixed-methods is appropriate when both orientations are needed

**Step 3: Evaluate Research Objectives Against Methodology Types**

Match objectives to approaches using these rules:
- **Quantitative** → measure, compare, test hypotheses, establish relationships, generalize findings, use existing instruments
- **Qualitative** → explore meaning, understand lived experience, generate theory, capture nuance, study underexplored phenomena
- **Mixed Methods** → validate one finding with another, explore before measuring, explain statistical results, or serve multiple stakeholder needs

**Step 4: Audit Practical Constraints**
- Sample size available: large (favors quantitative), small or purposive (favors qualitative)
- Data type accessible: numerical/structured (quantitative), text/narrative/observational (qualitative)
- Timeline: longitudinal studies may require quantitative; rapid deep inquiry suits qualitative
- Researcher skill set and available tools

**Step 5: Select and Justify a Specific Design**

Map to concrete designs within the recommended approach:
- Quantitative: survey, experiment, quasi-experiment, secondary data analysis, correlational
- Qualitative: grounded theory, phenomenology, ethnography, case study, narrative inquiry, thematic analysis
- Mixed: explanatory sequential, exploratory sequential, convergent parallel, embedded

**Step 6: Flag Epistemological Mismatches**
- Warn if the chosen question type conflicts with the researcher's stated preference (e.g., wanting to "prove" something with a phenomenological design)
- Identify if the question needs to be reframed to align with the methodology

## Output Format

Produce a structured recommendation with the following sections:

**Research Question Analysis**
- Restate the research question (refined if needed)
- Question type and key objectives (2–3 bullets)

**Recommended Methodology**
- Primary approach: Qualitative / Quantitative / Mixed Methods
- Specific design (e.g., "Semi-structured interviews analyzed via thematic analysis")
- Epistemological alignment: one sentence explaining why this fits

**Rationale**
- 3–5 bullet points explaining why this methodology matches the question, objectives, and context

**Trade-offs and Alternatives**
- One alternative methodology with a brief explanation of when to choose it instead
- Key limitation of the recommended approach

**Practical Next Steps**
- 3 concrete actions to begin designing the study (e.g., define sampling strategy, select instrument, identify IRB requirements)

Length: 350–600 words. Use headers and bullets. Avoid academic jargon unless the researcher is clearly working in an academic context.
