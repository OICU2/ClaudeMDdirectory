# Research Participant Incentive Design

This skill creates a complete, ethical incentive structure for research studies by analyzing study type, participant burden, budget, and compliance requirements to recommend compensation amounts, formats, and distribution logic.

## Incentive Design Workflow

### 1. Gather Study Parameters
Collect the following before recommending anything:
- **Study type**: survey, interview, diary study, usability test, longitudinal, clinical, etc.
- **Participant time burden**: total minutes/hours per session and number of sessions
- **Target population**: general public, professionals, patients, minors, vulnerable groups
- **Sample size goal** and current recruitment gap
- **Total compensation budget** (or ask if unknown)
- **Geographic scope**: country/region matters for legal and cultural norms
- **IRB or ethics board constraints**: any pre-approved limits or required disclosures

### 2. Calculate Baseline Fair Compensation
Use these anchors to estimate appropriate pay:
- **Minimum threshold**: match or exceed local minimum wage equivalent for time spent
- **Professional/expert participants**: multiply by 2–5x based on expertise scarcity (e.g., surgeons, CTOs)
- **Burden multiplier**: add 20–50% for tasks involving sensitive topics, physical discomfort, or cognitive load
- **Longitudinal multiplier**: increase per-session value by 10–15% for studies with 3+ sessions to reduce dropout
- **Hard-to-reach populations**: increase by 25–75% for low-incidence or highly screened groups

### 3. Choose Incentive Format
Match format to population and study constraints:

| Format | Best For | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|
| Cash / Venmo / PayPal | General public, fast studies | Tax reporting thresholds ($600 US) |
| Gift cards | Corporate or professional samples | Perceived lower value than cash |
| Amazon / retail vouchers | Online panels, remote studies | Geographic exclusion |
| Charitable donation in participant's name | Values-driven or altruistic populations | Lower completion than direct pay |
| Lottery / prize draw | Low-budget, large N surveys | Reduces response quality; use only for non-critical feedback |
| Points / credits | Platform-based research | Limits recruitment to platform users |
| Product / service access | Early adopter or beta user research | Must have genuine value; not a substitute for fair pay |

**Avoid lotteries as the primary incentive** when response quality matters — they inflate completion at the cost of thoughtfulness.

### 4. Design for Response Quality, Not Just Completion
Structure incentives to discourage speeding and satisficing:
- **Conditional bonuses**: offer a small base payment plus a bonus for responses that meet a quality threshold (e.g., open-text length, attention check passage)
- **Completion milestone payments**: for longitudinal studies, increase payment at each wave (e.g., $10, $15, $20 across three sessions)
- **Do not tie payment to specific answers**: this introduces acquiescence bias and violates most IRB standards
- **Disclose full payment structure upfront** in consent forms — partial disclosure inflates dropout when participants feel misled

### 5. Address Ethical Constraints
Flag and resolve these common issues:
- **Undue inducement**: if incentive is so high it could impair voluntary consent (especially in low-income or captive populations), reduce amount or add consent language
- **Coercion risk**: never make incentives contingent on particular answers or study outcomes
- **Vulnerable populations**: for minors, prisoners, or patients, follow IRB-specific rules — often token compensation only, or compensation goes to guardian
- **Tax compliance**: inform participants when individual payments may be reportable income; collect required tax forms (W-9 in US for payments over $600/year)
