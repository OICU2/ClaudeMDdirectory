# Research Findings to Policy Brief

Converts complex research into structured, jargon-free policy briefs that give decision-makers clear evidence summaries and actionable recommendations without sacrificing accuracy.

## Workflow

1. **Extract the core finding** — Identify the single most policy-relevant result. If multiple findings exist, rank them by decision-making impact, not statistical significance.

2. **Audit for jargon** — Flag every technical term, acronym, statistical measure (p-values, confidence intervals, effect sizes), and discipline-specific concept. Replace or define each one in plain language. Keep definitions to one clause maximum.

3. **Assess evidence strength honestly** — Characterize what the evidence can and cannot support. Distinguish correlation from causation, sample limitations, replication status, and any conflicting studies. Do not overstate certainty to make recommendations sound stronger.

4. **Identify the decision-relevant population** — Determine who is affected, at what scale, and over what timeframe. State this concretely (e.g., "approximately 2.3 million rural households" not "significant portions of the population").

5. **Derive policy options** — Generate 2–4 distinct policy responses ranging from minimal to robust intervention. For each option, state: what it requires, what evidence supports it, and what tradeoffs it involves.

6. **Anticipate objections** — Note the strongest counterarguments or implementation barriers a skeptical decision-maker would raise. Address each with evidence or honest acknowledgment of uncertainty.

7. **Strip academic hedging, add decision hedging** — Remove phrases like "further research is needed" unless specifying what research and why it matters. Add practical uncertainty flags: budget unknowns, political feasibility, timeline risks.

## Output Format

Produce a brief with these sections in order, each with a bolded heading:

- **Issue** (2–3 sentences): What problem does this research address and why does it matter now?
- **Key Finding** (3–5 sentences): What the research shows, in plain language, with honest characterization of evidence quality.
- **Who Is Affected** (2–3 sentences): Population, scale, and timeframe.
- **Policy Options** (bullet list of 2–4 options, each 3–5 sentences): Label each option (e.g., Option A: Targeted Subsidy). State action, evidence basis, and tradeoffs.
- **Recommended Next Step** (2–3 sentences): The single most defensible immediate action given current evidence, with rationale.
- **Limitations and Uncertainties** (bullet list, 3–5 items): What the evidence does not yet support or where implementation risk is high.
- **Source Summary** (1–2 sentences): Study type, sample, date, and any notable funding or conflict-of-interest disclosures.

Target length: 400–700 words. Use no headers beyond those listed. Use no citations in academic format — reference studies as "a 2023 randomized trial of 4,000 participants" not "(Smith et al., 2023)."
