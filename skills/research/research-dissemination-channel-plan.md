# Research Dissemination Channel Planner

This skill analyzes completed research findings and produces a prioritized, actionable plan for matching them to the right publication formats, target audiences, and distribution channels to maximize reach and impact.

## Dissemination Planning Workflow

### 1. Characterize the Research
- Identify the research domain, methodology, and type of findings (empirical, theoretical, review, applied)
- Assess novelty level: incremental advance, significant contribution, or field-changing
- Note any interdisciplinary angles, policy relevance, or public interest hooks
- Identify key constraints: timeline, open-access requirements, funder mandates, IP restrictions

### 2. Define Audience Segments
For each finding or output, identify at least three distinct audiences:
- **Primary academic audience**: Specialists in the exact subfield
- **Adjacent academic audience**: Researchers in related disciplines who would benefit
- **Non-academic audience**: Practitioners, policymakers, journalists, or the general public if applicable

### 3. Match Findings to Publication Formats
Select formats based on audience and finding type:
- **Peer-reviewed journal article**: For novel empirical findings or theoretical contributions — identify 3 ranked target journals with impact factor, scope fit, and open-access options
- **Conference paper or poster**: For early-stage findings or community engagement — identify 2–3 relevant upcoming conferences
- **Preprint**: For immediate visibility before peer review — recommend arXiv, bioRxiv, SSRN, or domain-appropriate server
- **Review or synthesis article**: If findings contextualize a body of literature
- **Policy brief or white paper**: If findings have direct governance or industry implications
- **Blog post or op-ed**: For public-facing findings — suggest specific outlets (e.g., The Conversation, domain-specific blogs)
- **Data or code repository**: If the artifact itself is the contribution — recommend Zenodo, GitHub, OSF, domain repositories
- **Press release or media pitch**: If findings are newsworthy

### 4. Prioritize and Sequence Channels
- Assign each channel a priority tier: Tier 1 (must do), Tier 2 (high value), Tier 3 (opportunistic)
- Map a realistic timeline with milestones (e.g., preprint in 2 weeks, journal submission in 6 weeks, conference abstract by deadline)
- Flag dependencies (e.g., don't post blog before journal embargo clears)

### 5. Tailor Messaging per Channel
- Draft a one-sentence "core claim" adapted for each audience
- Identify the hook for non-specialist audiences (practical implication, surprising result, societal relevance)
- Note what supplementary materials each channel requires (data availability statement, lay summary, visual abstract, supplementary code)

### 6. Identify Amplification Tactics
- Social media: Recommend platforms by discipline (e.g., Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Mastodon, ResearchGate)
- Email lists and listservs relevant to the field
- Collaboration with institutional communications office
- Engagement with relevant professional societies or working groups

## Output Format

Produce a structured dissemination plan with the following sections:

**Research Summary** (2–3 sentences): Core findings and their significance.

**Audience Map** (bulleted table or list): Three or more audience segments with their information needs and access preferences.

**Channel Plan** (prioritized table):
