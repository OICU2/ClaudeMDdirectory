# Research Gap Identification

This skill systematically maps what is known versus unknown in a research domain to surface actionable gaps, underexplored questions, and missing data — used whenever a user needs to locate opportunities for original contribution.

## Gap Identification Workflow

1. **Define the domain boundary** — Clarify the research area, time range, and scope (e.g., discipline, subdiscipline, population, geographic focus). Ask for clarification if the domain is ambiguous.

2. **Inventory what exists** — Catalogue major themes, methodologies, datasets, and findings already present in the provided literature, abstracts, or domain description. Group by recurrence and prominence.

3. **Apply six gap lenses** — Systematically check for gaps across:
   - **Population gaps** — Groups, demographics, or species underrepresented in studies
   - **Methodology gaps** — Research designs, instruments, or statistical approaches rarely applied to this domain
   - **Geographic/contextual gaps** — Regions, settings, or cultural contexts absent from the literature
   - **Temporal gaps** — Time periods not studied, lack of longitudinal data, or outdated foundational studies
   - **Conceptual gaps** — Theories untested, constructs unmeasured, or causal mechanisms unexamined
   - **Data gaps** — Missing variables, unavailable datasets, low sample sizes, or poor replication

4. **Rank by evidence strength** — Classify each identified gap as:
   - **High-confidence gap**: explicitly noted as a limitation in multiple sources
   - **Inferred gap**: logically absent given the landscape but not directly stated
   - **Speculative gap**: plausible but requires further verification

5. **Generate research questions** — For each significant gap, write one concrete, answerable research question that would address it.

6. **Flag feasibility constraints** — Note which gaps are likely blocked by ethical barriers, data unavailability, cost, or technological limitations.

## Output Format

Produce a structured report with the following sections:

**Domain Summary** (2–3 sentences): What the field currently covers and its dominant focus.

**Identified Gaps** (bulleted list per lens):
- Lens name in bold
- One-sentence description of the gap
- Confidence level in brackets: [High / Inferred / Speculative]
- One concrete research question stemming from this gap

**Priority Gaps** (top 3–5): Ranked list of the most significant gaps with a one-sentence rationale for each ranking.

**Feasibility Notes**: Brief flags on any gaps that face structural barriers to investigation.

Length: Scale to input complexity — 400–600 words for narrow domains, up to 1,200 words for broad or multi-source inputs. Use headers and bullets throughout; no dense prose paragraphs.
