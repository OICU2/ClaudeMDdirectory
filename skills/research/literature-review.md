# Literature Review

Conducts a structured survey of existing research on a topic, synthesizing key findings, methodologies, and debates — activated when users need an evidence-based overview of a field or question.

## Review Workflow

1. **Define the scope** — Clarify the research question, time range, and relevant disciplines before proceeding. Ask if ambiguous.
2. **Identify major themes** — Organize the literature into 3–6 thematic clusters (e.g., theoretical frameworks, empirical findings, contested claims, methodological approaches).
3. **Assess the evidence base** — For each theme, note study types (RCT, observational, meta-analysis, qualitative), sample sizes, and strength of evidence. Flag weak or outdated evidence explicitly.
4. **Synthesize across sources** — Do not summarize sources one by one. Draw cross-cutting conclusions, note where findings converge or conflict, and explain *why* disagreements exist (different populations, methods, definitions).
5. **Identify gaps and debates** — State what remains understudied, what methodological limitations recur, and where scholarly consensus is absent.
6. **Surface key citations** — Name landmark studies, seminal authors, or foundational papers by name where known. Flag when specific citations are uncertain rather than fabricating references.
7. **State implications** — Conclude with what the literature collectively suggests for practice, policy, or future research.

## Output Format

Produce a structured review with the following sections:

**Overview** (2–3 sentences): The research question, scope, and volume/maturity of the literature.

**Thematic Sections** (one H3 heading per theme, 1–3 paragraphs each): Synthesized findings with evidence quality noted inline. Use phrases like "strong evidence suggests," "limited evidence indicates," or "findings are mixed."

**Points of Debate** (bullet list): 3–5 active controversies or unresolved questions, each with a one-sentence explanation of why the disagreement persists.

**Research Gaps** (bullet list): 3–5 specific understudied areas or methodological limitations.

**Summary and Implications** (1 paragraph): What the literature collectively concludes and what it means for the user's context.

**Key References** (list): Named studies or authors where confidently known; marked `[verify]` if uncertain.

Total length: 600–1200 words depending on topic complexity. Use plain prose, not bullet-heavy summaries, within thematic sections.
