# Cross-Study Synthesis

This skill aggregates findings from multiple independent studies into a structured synthesis that maps consensus, conflict, and evidence strength across the full body of work.

## Synthesis Workflow

1. **Extract core claims** from each study: identify the primary finding, population/context, methodology, sample size, and any reported effect sizes or confidence levels.

2. **Group findings by theme or outcome**: cluster studies that address the same variable, question, or intervention into discrete topic buckets before comparing.

3. **Map agreement and disagreement**: for each theme, explicitly note which studies align, which contradict, and whether contradictions are explained by methodology, population, timeframe, or measurement differences.

4. **Assess cumulative evidence weight**: consider number of studies, study quality (RCT > observational > anecdotal), sample sizes, replication, and recency. Flag when a majority finding rests on weak or homogeneous evidence.

5. **Identify gaps**: note what the studies collectively fail to address — unmeasured populations, unexplored variables, or questions that no study directly answers.

6. **State the synthesis verdict**: produce a single plain-language conclusion that reflects the balance of evidence, with confidence level (strong / moderate / limited / contested).

## Output Format

Produce a structured synthesis with these sections:

**Studies Reviewed** — bulleted list of each source with one-line description (author/year, topic, method, n if known).

**Themes and Findings** — one subsection per major theme. Each subsection contains:
- Agreements: what studies consistently show
- Contradictions: where studies diverge and likely reasons why
- Evidence weight: strong / moderate / limited with 1-sentence justification

**Synthesis Verdict** — 2–4 sentences summarizing the overall state of knowledge. State confidence level explicitly.

**Key Gaps** — bulleted list of what the collective evidence does not yet answer.

Length scales with number of studies: 3–5 studies = ~400 words; 6–12 studies = ~700 words; 12+ studies = structured table plus narrative.
