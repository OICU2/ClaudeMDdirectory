# Data Interpretation

This skill transforms raw numbers, datasets, and statistical outputs into plain-language conclusions and concrete next steps that non-experts can act on.

## Interpretation Workflow

1. **Audit the data first** — Identify data type (quantitative, qualitative, time-series, categorical), sample size, source, and any stated collection methodology before drawing conclusions.

2. **Flag data quality issues** — Note missing values, outliers, small sample sizes, potential biases, or collection inconsistencies that could affect reliability of conclusions.

3. **Identify patterns and relationships** — Look for trends over time, correlations between variables, distributions, anomalies, and comparisons against baselines or benchmarks when available.

4. **Rank findings by significance** — Separate findings that are statistically or practically significant from noise. State confidence level explicitly (high/medium/low) based on data quality and sample size.

5. **Translate to plain language** — Restate each key finding in one sentence a non-specialist can understand, avoiding jargon unless defined.

6. **Connect findings to context** — Relate the data back to the original question or goal the user was trying to answer.

7. **Generate actionable recommendations** — For each major finding, produce at least one concrete recommendation with a stated rationale tied directly to the data.

8. **State limitations honestly** — Specify what the data cannot tell you, what additional data would strengthen conclusions, and where interpretation involves assumption.

## Output Format

Produce a structured report with these sections:

**Data Summary** (2–4 sentences): What the dataset contains, its scope, and any quality flags.

**Key Findings** (bulleted list, 3–7 items): Each finding stated as a single declarative sentence with confidence level in brackets — e.g., *Conversion rates dropped 18% in Q3 [High confidence — full dataset, n=12,400]*.

**Interpretation**: 1–3 short paragraphs explaining what the findings mean in context, including any notable relationships between findings.

**Recommendations** (numbered list): Concrete, specific actions tied to findings. Each item follows the format: *Action → Rationale → Expected outcome*.

**Limitations & Caveats** (bulleted list): What the data doesn't cover, assumptions made, and what additional information would improve confidence.

Keep total length proportional to data complexity — simple datasets warrant 300–400 words; complex multi-variable datasets may require 600–900 words.
