# Research Reporting

This skill converts complex research findings into structured, accessible reports that non-technical audiences can read, understand, and act on.

## Reporting Workflow

1. **Identify the audience** — Ask or infer who will read this: executives, clients, general public, policymakers. Tailor vocabulary and assumed knowledge accordingly.
2. **Extract the core finding** — Identify the single most important takeaway. Every section of the report should connect back to it.
3. **Structure before writing** — Use this order: Executive Summary → Background → Key Findings → What It Means → Limitations → Recommendations.
4. **Translate jargon** — Replace every technical term with plain language. If a term must stay, define it in one sentence the first time it appears.
5. **Lead with conclusions** — State findings upfront. Non-technical readers should not have to read to the end to learn what was discovered.
6. **Use concrete numbers** — Replace vague language ("significant increase") with specific figures ("37% increase over six months").
7. **Explain the "so what"** — For each finding, explicitly state why it matters and what action or decision it informs.
8. **Flag uncertainty honestly** — Note confidence levels, sample sizes, or data gaps in plain terms without undermining the report's credibility.

## Output Format

Produce a complete report with these clearly labeled sections:

**Executive Summary** (3–5 sentences): The core finding, why it matters, and the top recommendation. Written for someone who will read nothing else.

**Background** (1–2 short paragraphs): Context for the research — what question was being answered and why it was asked.

**Key Findings** (bullet list or numbered, 3–7 items): Each finding stated as a clear declarative sentence with supporting data inline.

**What This Means** (1–2 paragraphs): Interpretation for a non-expert. Connects findings to real-world implications.

**Limitations** (3–5 bullets): Honest, plain-language caveats about data quality, scope, or methodology.

**Recommendations** (numbered list, 2–5 items): Specific, actionable next steps tied directly to the findings.

**Length**: 400–900 words for the full report unless the source material requires more. Use headers, bullets, and bold text to aid scanning. No footnotes, no passive voice, no academic hedging.
