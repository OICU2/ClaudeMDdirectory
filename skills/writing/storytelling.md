# Storytelling Skill

This skill applies narrative structure — character, tension, and resolution — to make any idea, data, or message emotionally resonant and memorable.

## Narrative Construction Process

1. **Identify the core truth** — Strip the content to its single most important idea. Everything else serves this.

2. **Find or create a protagonist** — Assign a human (or human-like) perspective: a customer, an employee, a researcher, a community. Readers follow people, not concepts.

3. **Establish the before-state** — Describe the world or situation before the central change or challenge. Ground it in specific, sensory detail. Avoid generalities.

4. **Introduce the conflict or tension** — Name the obstacle, risk, question, or gap that creates forward momentum. Make the stakes clear and real.

5. **Show the turning point** — Identify the moment of decision, discovery, or change. This is the hinge of the story. It must feel earned, not arbitrary.

6. **Deliver the resolution and meaning** — Resolve the tension and explicitly connect the outcome back to the core truth. Leave the reader with one clear takeaway.

7. **Apply the right narrative mode** — Choose the appropriate form:
   - *Anecdote*: short, single scene, first or third person
   - *Case study arc*: problem → approach → result → lesson
   - *Hero's journey*: challenge → transformation → new capability
   - *Data story*: anomaly → investigation → insight → implication

8. **Cut what doesn't serve the story** — Remove any detail that doesn't develop character, advance conflict, or reinforce the core truth.

## Output Format

Produce a structured narrative with the following:

- **Opening hook** (1–3 sentences): Drop into a specific moment, scene, or provocative statement — no throat-clearing
- **Body** (3–6 paragraphs): Follow the constructed arc with concrete details, active voice, and varied sentence rhythm
- **Resolution** (1–2 sentences): Close the loop and land the meaning clearly
- **Narrative note** (optional, italicized): If adapting factual content, briefly note what was invented or compressed for narrative effect

Length scales to purpose:
- Micro-story or pitch moment: 100–200 words
- Explainer or presentation narrative: 300–600 words
- Full case study or essay: 600–1200 words

Format is flowing prose unless a scene-break structure (using `---`) better serves pacing.
