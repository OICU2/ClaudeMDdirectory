# Narrative Development

This skill constructs layered story architectures with clear conflict escalation, character transformation, and thematic coherence, activating whenever a writer needs help shaping a narrative from premise to payoff.

## Story Architecture Workflow

### 1. Establish the Core Premise
- Identify the central character and their dominant desire
- Define the opposing force (internal, external, or systemic)
- State the thematic question the story will answer

### 2. Map the Five Structural Pillars
- **Opening Hook**: Introduce the world and disrupt its equilibrium within the first 10%
- **Inciting Incident**: Force the protagonist into irreversible action
- **Midpoint Shift**: Raise stakes or reverse the situation — never let the middle coast
- **Dark Night / Crisis**: Strip the protagonist of their last safety net before the climax
- **Resolution**: Answer the thematic question and show how the protagonist has changed

### 3. Build Scene-Level Momentum
- Every scene must change the story's direction (up or down on the tension axis)
- Alternate between action scenes and reaction/processing scenes
- Plant setup elements at least two beats before their payoff
- End scenes on a question, not an answer — pull readers forward

### 4. Layer Subplots Purposefully
- Each subplot must mirror, contrast, or complicate the main theme
- Subplots should intersect the main plot at the midpoint and climax
- Limit active subplots to two or three to avoid narrative dilution

### 5. Diagnose Structural Problems
- Sagging middle: Insert a false victory or unexpected reversal at the 60% mark
- Weak ending: Trace back to find an unresolved setup — the resolution lives there
- Flat character arc: Define what the protagonist believes at the start and must unlearn by the end
- Pacing drag: Cut any scene that neither advances plot nor reveals character

## Output Format

Produce a structured narrative development plan with the following sections:

1. **Premise Statement** (2–3 sentences: character, desire, obstacle, theme)
2. **Five-Pillar Outline** (one paragraph per pillar, noting approximate story percentage)
3. **Scene Beat Sheet** (bullet list of 10–20 beats in sequence, each labeled with its function)
4. **Subplot Map** (table: subplot name | thematic role | intersection points with main plot)
5. **Tension Graph** (text-based arc showing rises and falls across the structure)
6. **Structural Notes** (flagged weaknesses and specific fixes)

Length scales with project scope: short story plans run 400–600 words; novel outlines run 800–1,500 words.
