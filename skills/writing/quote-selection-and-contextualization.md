# Quote Selection and Contextualization

This skill reviews raw interview transcripts or source material, selects the strongest quotes based on clarity, specificity, and narrative value, and generates framing sentences that integrate each quote into a draft.

## Quote Selection and Framing Workflow

1. **Ingest the material**: Read the full transcript or source text before selecting anything. Note the topic, speaker, and apparent purpose of the piece.

2. **Identify quote candidates**: Flag passages that meet at least two of these criteria:
   - Contains a specific detail, number, or concrete example
   - Expresses a strong opinion, emotion, or unique perspective
   - Advances the narrative or argument of the piece
   - Uses vivid, distinctive, or memorable language
   - Reveals character or voice in a way summary cannot

3. **Cut weak quotes**: Remove candidates that are vague, purely factual (better paraphrased), or redundant with stronger options already selected.

4. **Rank by function**: Categorize surviving quotes by their role — opening hook, supporting evidence, counterpoint, emotional beat, or closing resonance.

5. **Write framing sentences**: For each selected quote, write one lead-in sentence and, if needed, one follow-up sentence. The lead-in must:
   - Identify the speaker by name and relevant credential on first use
   - Signal the quote's significance without restating it
   - Fit grammatically with the quoted text

6. **Flag edits needed**: If a quote requires ellipsis, brackets, or condensing for clarity, note the original alongside the edited version and explain the change.

7. **Check diversity**: Ensure the selected quotes represent a range of tones and functions — avoid selecting five quotes that all do the same rhetorical work.

## Output Format

Produce a numbered list of quote blocks. Each block contains:

```
**Quote [#] — [Function: e.g., Opening Hook / Supporting Evidence / Emotional Beat]**

Framing sentence: [Lead-in sentence here.]

Quote: "[Exact quoted text, with any edits bracketed]"

Follow-up (if needed): [One sentence placing the quote in context or bridging to the next point.]

Note: [Flag any edits made, sourcing ambiguities, or suggested placement in the draft.]
```

- Aim to surface 5–10 quotes from a standard transcript; scale up for longer source material
- Preserve the speaker's voice — do not smooth out dialect or informal register unless asked
- If no quotes meet the selection criteria, say so explicitly and explain what is missing from the source material
