# Metaphor and Analogy Development

This skill generates, evaluates, and refines metaphors and analogies that translate complex or abstract concepts into terms a specific audience will instantly grasp.

## Workflow

1. **Extract the core concept** — Identify the single most important truth about the concept that the audience must understand. Strip away secondary details.

2. **Profile the audience** — Determine what the audience already knows, their domain, their daily experiences, and what analogical territory feels familiar to them (e.g., cooking, sports, construction, parenting).

3. **Generate multiple candidate analogies** — Produce at least 3–5 distinct metaphors or analogies from different domains. Do not stop at the first obvious comparison.

4. **Stress-test each candidate** — For each analogy, ask:
   - Where does it hold? List 2–3 strong mapping points.
   - Where does it break down? Identify at least one failure point.
   - Could it mislead the audience into a wrong mental model?

5. **Select and extend the strongest analogy** — Choose the one with the most accurate mappings and fewest misleading implications. Develop it into a full explanation using the analogy as a scaffold.

6. **Add a breakdown disclaimer if needed** — If the chosen analogy has a significant limitation, briefly note where the comparison stops working so the audience doesn't over-apply it.

7. **Offer alternatives** — Provide 1–2 runner-up analogies with brief notes on when they would be more appropriate (e.g., for a different audience or a different aspect of the concept).

## Output Format

Produce a structured response with the following sections:

**Core Concept (1 sentence):** The distilled idea being explained.

**Primary Analogy:**
- The analogy stated plainly in 1–2 sentences.
- Mapping table or bullet list showing 3 explicit parallels between the analogy and the concept.
- Where it breaks down: 1–2 sentences noting the analogy's limits.
- Full explanation (2–4 sentences) using the analogy to walk through the concept naturally.

**Runner-Up Analogies (2 options):**
- Each stated in 1 sentence with a note on best use case.

**Usage Note (optional):** 1 sentence on how to introduce the analogy in context (e.g., "leads well into a live demo" or "works best in writing, not spoken explanation").
