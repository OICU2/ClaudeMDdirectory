# Interview Transcription Writing

This skill converts raw interview transcripts into polished written content, preserving the subject's voice and key insights while eliminating filler, redundancy, and conversational noise.

## Workflow

1. **Identify the output format** — Ask or infer whether the user wants a Q&A, narrative profile, article with quotes, or another format before proceeding.
2. **Extract the spine** — Read the full transcript and identify the 3–7 most compelling moments, insights, or quotes that should anchor the piece.
3. **Determine voice and tone** — Match the publication style or audience context (journalistic, casual, academic, brand-forward) based on user context or by asking directly.
4. **Strip the noise** — Remove filler words (um, uh, you know, like), false starts, repeated statements, and off-topic tangents. Never alter the meaning of what was said.
5. **Reconstruct for readability** — Reorder content thematically if needed; interviews rarely flow in the best narrative order. Group related ideas together.
6. **Write transitions and framing** — Add a compelling lede, section headers if appropriate, and connective tissue between quotes or sections. The writer's voice frames; the subject's voice carries.
7. **Preserve authenticity** — Keep distinctive phrases, cadence, and vocabulary that reflect how the subject actually speaks. Do not over-polish quotes into generic prose.
8. **Flag direct quotes** — Any sentence presented as a direct quote must come verbatim (or near-verbatim with light cleanup) from the transcript. Do not fabricate quotes.

## Output Format

Produce the final written piece in full, ready to publish or hand to an editor. Include:

- **Lede or opening paragraph** that hooks the reader and establishes the subject and stakes
- **Body** structured thematically or chronologically depending on format, with integrated quotes and paraphrased context
- **Closing** that lands on a strong final quote or takeaway
- **Format tags** if the user requested a specific format (e.g., bold questions for Q&A, section headers for a long-form profile)

Length should match the depth of the source material — typically 400–1,200 words for a profile or article, shorter for a Q&A excerpt. If the transcript is long, note which sections were cut and why.
