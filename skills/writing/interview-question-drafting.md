# Interview Question Drafting

This skill generates a structured, sequenced set of interview questions designed to draw out usable quotes, reveal story angles, and guide a journalist or writer through a productive conversation with their subject.

## Workflow

1. **Gather subject profile** — Collect the subject's name, role, background, notable work, public statements, and any known controversies or tensions. If the user hasn't provided this, ask before proceeding.

2. **Clarify article objective** — Identify the editorial angle: profile piece, news hook, opinion feature, Q&A format, or investigative story. If unclear, ask the user to state the publication, audience, and desired reader takeaway in one sentence.

3. **Identify story angles** — Before writing questions, list 3–5 potential story angles the interview could support (e.g., origin story, inflection point, contrarian view, human cost, future stakes). Surface these to the user and confirm which to pursue.

4. **Sequence the questions by phase:**
   - **Warm-up (2–3 questions):** Low-stakes, rapport-building questions about background or recent work. These loosen the subject and establish baseline voice.
   - **Core narrative (4–6 questions):** Questions targeting the main story angle. Use open-ended framing ("Walk me through…", "What was the moment when…") to invite storytelling, not yes/no answers.
   - **Quote-bait (3–4 questions):** Provocative or specific questions designed to produce a memorable, shareable line. Include at least one that introduces a tension or opposing view for the subject to respond to.
   - **Forward-looking (2–3 questions):** Questions about implications, predictions, or what the subject wants readers to understand. These produce closing quotes and editorial hooks.
   - **Catch-all (1–2 questions):** Open invitations ("Is there something I haven't asked that matters here?") to surface material the journalist didn't know to request.

5. **Write each question as a final draft** — Questions must be complete sentences, specific to the subject, and free of jargon. Avoid compound questions. Flag any question that may require follow-up prompts and provide the follow-up in brackets.

6. **Annotate intent** — After each question or phase, add a one-line note explaining what usable output the question is designed to extract (e.g., "targets origin story," "designed to surface a regret," "intended to produce a forward-looking pull quote").

## Output Format

Produce a numbered question list organized under the five phase headers (Warm-up, Core Narrative, Quote-Bait, Forward-Looking, Catch-All). Each question appears on its own line. Inline follow-up prompts appear in brackets immediately after the question. Annotation notes appear in italics below each question.

Before the question list, include a short **Story Angles** block listing the 3–5 angles identified in step 3, with the pursued angle marked.

Total output: 12–18 questions. Length scales with complexity of subject — a first-time profile warrants fewer questions than an investigative interview. Do not include filler questions that could apply to any subject.
