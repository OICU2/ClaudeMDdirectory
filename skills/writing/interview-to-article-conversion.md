# Interview to Article Conversion

This skill takes raw interview transcripts or Q&A exchanges and produces polished, publication-ready articles with strong narrative structure, extracted key insights, and a consistent editorial voice.

## Conversion Workflow

1. **Read the full transcript first** — scan the entire interview before writing anything; identify the 3–5 most substantive claims, insights, or arguments the subject makes.

2. **Identify the core thesis** — determine what the interview is ultimately about; every article needs a single governing idea (e.g., "why this founder believes remote work is permanently broken" not "an interview with a founder").

3. **Extract quotable material** — pull 4–8 direct quotes that are vivid, specific, or provocative; discard filler, throat-clearing, and repetition; never paraphrase a quote and present it in quotation marks.

4. **Build a structure before writing:**
   - **Lede** (1–2 sentences): the most surprising or compelling point from the interview; do not start with "In this interview..."
   - **Context paragraph**: who the subject is and why their perspective matters — keep to 2–3 sentences
   - **Body sections** (2–4): each organized around a distinct theme or argument, not chronologically by how the interview unfolded
   - **Closing**: end on the subject's forward-looking statement, strongest conviction, or a tension left unresolved

5. **Write in third person unless told otherwise** — refer to the interview subject by name or role; use present tense for their views ("She argues that..."), past tense for events.

6. **Integrate quotes naturally** — quotes should advance the argument, not summarize what the surrounding prose already said; introduce each quote with context, not a generic "she said."

7. **Cut what doesn't serve the article** — off-topic tangents, interviewer questions (unless essential), redundant examples, and conversational filler do not belong in the final piece.

8. **Match the publication register** — if the user specifies an outlet or tone (trade publication, mainstream magazine, blog), adjust sentence length, formality, and assumed reader expertise accordingly; if unspecified, default to clear, intelligent general-audience prose.

## Output Format

Produce the article in this structure:

**[Headline]** — specific, active, no more than 12 words; avoids generic phrasing like "A Conversation With..."

**[Subheadline]** — one sentence expanding on the headline's promise (optional, include if natural)

**[Body]** — 400–900 words unless a target length is specified; broken into paragraphs of 3–5 sentences; no H2 headers unless the piece is long-form (800+ words); quotes integrated inline, not set apart as block quotes unless stylistically appropriate.

**[End]** — no sign-off, no "conclusion" label; the final paragraph closes the narrative.

After the article, provide a brief **Editor's Note** (italicized, separated by a horizontal rule) with:
- Suggested word count if further trimming is needed
- 1–2 alternative headline options
- Any gaps in the transcript that weakened the article (e.g., "The subject's claim about market size is unverified — recommend flagging for fact-check")
