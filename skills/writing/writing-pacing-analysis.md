# Writing Pacing Analysis

This skill dissects a draft's rhythmic and structural pacing by measuring sentence length variation, section density, and momentum shifts, then delivers a diagnostic report with targeted fixes.

## Pacing Analysis Workflow

1. **Segment the draft** — Divide the text into logical sections (paragraphs, scenes, chapters, or every 200–300 words if no natural breaks exist).

2. **Measure sentence length distribution per section** — Count short (1–8 words), medium (9–20 words), and long (21+ words) sentences. Flag sections where one category exceeds 70% of sentences as monotone zones.

3. **Calculate section density** — Identify whether each section is action-heavy (dialogue, movement, short sentences), reflective (exposition, description, long sentences), or balanced. Note consecutive same-density sections as pacing drags.

4. **Locate momentum breaks** — Find where forward motion stalls: back-to-back long paragraphs, repeated sentence openers, excessive adverbs/qualifiers, or scenes that resolve tension too quickly or too slowly.

5. **Map the pacing arc** — Sketch whether the overall piece follows a coherent tension curve (buildup → peak → release) or flatlines. Identify the highest and lowest energy points.

6. **Generate targeted recommendations** — For each flagged zone, prescribe a specific fix: split a long sentence, add a punchy one-liner, break up a dense paragraph, cut a transitional scene, or accelerate dialogue.

## Output Format

Produce a structured diagnostic report with these sections:

**Pacing Summary** (2–3 sentences): Overall rhythm verdict — fast, slow, uneven, or well-varied.

**Section-by-Section Breakdown** (table or bullet list):
- Section label or line range
- Dominant sentence type (short/medium/long %)
- Density tag (action / reflective / balanced)
- Pacing flag (drag / rush / monotone / healthy)

**Momentum Map** (1 short paragraph): Describe the energy arc across the full piece, naming where it peaks and where it stalls.

**Top 5 Fixes** (numbered list): Specific, actionable edits tied to exact sections — quote the problem sentence or passage, then show or describe the revision.

**One-Line Verdict**: A single sentence the writer can use to summarize what their pacing needs most right now.
