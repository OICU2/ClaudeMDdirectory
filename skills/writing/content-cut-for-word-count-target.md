# Content Cut for Word Count Target

This skill systematically reduces a draft to a specified word count by identifying and removing low-value content first, then compressing sentences, while preserving the argument structure and essential evidence.

## Cutting Workflow

1. **Confirm the target**: Identify the current word count and the target word count. Calculate the reduction needed (absolute and percentage).

2. **Audit for low-value content first** — remove in this priority order:
   - Throat-clearing introductions and redundant closing summaries
   - Repeated points stated more than once across the draft
   - Hedging phrases ("it is worth noting that," "in many ways," "it could be argued")
   - Transitional filler ("Moving on," "As we can see," "With that said")
   - Adverbs and intensifiers that add no meaning ("very," "quite," "extremely")
   - Over-explained examples — keep the example, cut the re-explanation

3. **Compress at the sentence level**:
   - Replace multi-clause sentences with direct ones
   - Convert passive voice to active voice where it shortens the sentence
   - Replace noun-heavy phrases with verbs ("make a decision" → "decide")
   - Combine two short related sentences into one

4. **Compress at the paragraph level**:
   - Merge paragraphs that make the same point in slightly different ways
   - Cut supporting evidence to the single strongest example per claim
   - Reduce scene-setting or context to the minimum needed for comprehension

5. **Preserve non-negotiables**:
   - The central thesis or argument
   - Each distinct claim that the conclusion depends on
   - Proper nouns, statistics, and citations that ground the piece
   - The opening hook and closing statement

6. **Check the count** after each pass. If still over target, repeat step 3–4 before touching step 5 items.

## Output Format

Produce the following in order:

**Cut Stats**
- Original word count: [N]
- Target word count: [N]
- Words removed: [N] ([X]% reduction)

**Revised Draft**
The full trimmed text, ready to use. No annotations or inline comments inside the draft itself.

**What Was Cut**
A brief bulleted list (5–10 items) of the specific types of cuts made — e.g., "Removed repeated thesis restatement in paragraph 3," "Compressed three examples to one in the evidence section," "Eliminated hedging language throughout." This helps the writer understand the editing logic and accept or reverse specific choices.
