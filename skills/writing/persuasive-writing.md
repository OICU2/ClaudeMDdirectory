# Persuasive Writing

This skill constructs audience-targeted arguments using classical rhetoric and modern persuasion principles, producing content that earns agreement and motivates action.

## Persuasion Workflow

1. **Clarify the goal**: Identify the single action or belief shift you want the reader to adopt. If unclear, ask before writing.
2. **Profile the audience**: Determine what the reader already believes, what objections they hold, and what they value most. Tailor every argument to that profile.
3. **Lead with the stakes**: Open by making the reader feel the cost of inaction or the reward of agreement — before presenting any argument.
4. **Structure the argument (3-part core)**:
   - **Logos**: Present the strongest factual claim or statistic that supports your position. Cite a credible source or frame it as verifiable.
   - **Pathos**: Include one concrete story, analogy, or vivid scenario that makes the abstract personal and emotionally real.
   - **Ethos**: Establish credibility — shared values, relevant experience, or acknowledgment of the counterargument before dismantling it.
5. **Steelman the opposition**: State the best version of the opposing view in one sentence, then refute it directly. This builds trust and disarms dismissal.
6. **Use concession strategically**: Acknowledge one legitimate limitation of your position. Readers trust writers who don't oversell.
7. **End with a specific call to action**: Name the exact next step — sign, share, reconsider, try, buy, vote, reply. Make it frictionless and immediate.
8. **Apply language techniques throughout**:
   - Use second-person ("you") to create direct address
   - Favor active voice and short declarative sentences for key claims
   - Repeat the core thesis in varied phrasing at least three times
   - Use tricolon (lists of three) for rhythm and memorability

## Output Format

Produce the persuasive piece in the format requested (essay, email, speech, op-ed, pitch, social post). Default to essay if unspecified.

**Structure:**
- **Hook** (1–3 sentences): stakes or provocative claim
- **Thesis** (1 sentence): the position being argued
- **Body** (3–5 paragraphs): one argument per paragraph, each with a claim, evidence, and bridge to the reader's values
- **Counterargument + rebuttal** (1 paragraph)
- **Call to action** (1–3 sentences): specific, urgent, frictionless

**Length:** 300–600 words for standard pieces; adjust only if a specific format demands otherwise (e.g., tweet thread = shorter, policy brief = longer).

**Tone:** Match to audience — formal for institutional audiences, conversational for general readers, urgent for advocacy, aspirational for sales.

After the draft, add a one-line note identifying the primary persuasion lever used (e.g., "Primary lever: fear of loss" or "Primary lever: social proof") so the writer can evaluate alignment with their intent.
