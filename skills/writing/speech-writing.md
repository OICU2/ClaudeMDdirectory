# Speech Writing

This skill writes complete, delivery-ready speeches calibrated to the speaker's voice, the audience's expectations, and the event's emotional register.

## Speech Writing Workflow

1. **Establish context first** — Identify the event type (keynote, toast, commencement, TED-style talk, eulogy, pitch), audience size and familiarity, and delivery format (live, recorded, teleprompter, memorized).

2. **Clarify the core purpose** — Every speech does one of three things: informs, inspires, or persuades. Pin down which, then identify the single most important thing the audience should feel or do after hearing it.

3. **Capture the speaker's voice** — Ask for or infer the speaker's natural tone (formal, conversational, humorous, authoritative). The speech must sound like the speaker, not like a generic template.

4. **Build the structure**:
   - **Opening hook** — Story, provocative question, bold statement, or vivid image. No "Hi, my name is" openings.
   - **Stakes** — Why this moment and this audience matter.
   - **Body** — 2–4 main points, each anchored by a concrete example, story, or data point.
   - **Turn** — A moment of shift, insight, or emotional crescendo before the close.
   - **Closing call** — A memorable final line or explicit call to action that echoes the opening.

5. **Write for the ear, not the eye** — Use short sentences, active voice, repetition for emphasis (anaphora, rule of three), and natural pause points. Avoid jargon, dense clauses, and anything that reads well but sounds awkward aloud.

6. **Mark delivery cues** — Insert [PAUSE], [slow down], or emphasis notes in brackets so the speaker knows where to breathe and land key lines.

7. **Time the speech** — Estimate delivery time at roughly 130 words per minute for conversational pace. Flag if the draft runs over or under the requested duration.

## Output Format

Produce the complete speech as a ready-to-deliver document with:

- **Header block**: Event, audience, estimated duration, tone descriptor
- **Full speech text**: Written in paragraphs as the speaker would deliver them, not bullet points
- **Delivery notes**: Inline in [brackets] for pauses, emphasis, or pacing
- **Word count and time estimate** at the bottom
- **Optional**: 1–3 alternate opening lines if the hook is high-stakes

Length scales with the request — a wedding toast runs 200–400 words; a keynote runs 1,200–2,500 words. Default to the requested duration or, if none given, ask before drafting anything over five minutes.
