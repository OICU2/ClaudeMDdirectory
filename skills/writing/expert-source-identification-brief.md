# Expert Source Identification Brief

This skill produces a prioritized, actionable source brief for writers beginning research on a specific article topic, identifying the exact experts, institutions, databases, and publications most likely to yield credible, quotable material.

## Source Identification Workflow

1. **Parse the topic**: Extract the core subject, relevant sub-disciplines, industry sector, and any geographic or temporal constraints implied by the article angle.

2. **Identify expert categories**: For the given topic, determine which expert types are most relevant — academics/researchers, practitioners/industry professionals, policy makers, critics/contrarians, and affected community members or primary sources.

3. **Name specific experts**: List 4–6 named individuals with their title, institutional affiliation, and why they are authoritative on this exact topic. Prioritize people who have published recently, testified publicly, or are frequently cited in reputable outlets.

4. **Identify key institutions and organizations**: List 3–5 organizations (universities, think tanks, agencies, NGOs, industry bodies) that produce primary research or data on this topic. Note what type of material each provides.

5. **Surface primary databases and documents**: Name specific databases, government repositories, court records, FOIA-accessible files, or data sets a journalist or writer should query directly.

6. **List target publications and beats**: Identify 4–6 publications — academic journals, trade outlets, or news organizations — that cover this beat closely and whose archives would yield background, prior coverage, and potential source leads.

7. **Flag potential bias or conflict of interest**: For each major source category, note any known funding relationships, advocacy positions, or institutional biases the writer should disclose or weigh.

8. **Suggest 2–3 unconventional or overlooked sources**: Include sources outside the obvious circle — local experts, international perspectives, historical archives, or adjacent fields that illuminate the topic from an unexpected angle.

## Output Format

Produce a structured brief with the following sections, using headers and bullet points:

**Topic & Angle Summary** (1–2 sentences confirming interpretation of the article focus)

**Named Experts to Contact**
- Name, Title, Institution — Why relevant — Best contact avenue (published email, university page, Twitter/LinkedIn, publicist)

**Key Institutions & What They Provide**
- Institution name — Type of resource (data, reports, spokespeople, archives)

**Primary Data Sources & Databases**
- Source name — What it contains — How to access

**Essential Publications & Archives**
- Publication/journal — Relevant beat or section — Suggested search terms

**Bias & Conflict-of-Interest Flags**
- Brief notes on any sources with known advocacy positions or funding ties

**Overlooked or Unconventional Sources**
- 2–3 sources outside the obvious tier with a one-line rationale each

Length: 400–700 words. Prioritize specificity over comprehensiveness — a shorter list of precisely relevant sources outperforms a long generic one.
