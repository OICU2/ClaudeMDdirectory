# Op-Ed Argument Scaffolder

This skill builds a complete argumentative scaffold for an opinion piece, mapping a central thesis to evidence, counterarguments, and a call to action so the writer has a ready-to-draft outline.

## Scaffolding Workflow

1. **Extract the central claim.** Ask the user to state their core position in one sentence if they haven't already. Sharpen vague positions into a specific, contestable thesis (e.g., not "social media is bad" but "Algorithmic feeds should carry a legal duty of care for minors").

2. **Identify the audience and publication context.** Ask who will read this and where it might run (general newspaper, trade publication, blog). Tailor the register and assumed knowledge accordingly.

3. **Generate three supporting pillars.** Produce three distinct argument types:
   - **Empirical:** data, research, or documented examples that support the claim.
   - **Logical/Causal:** a cause-and-effect or principled reasoning chain.
   - **Anecdotal/Human:** a concrete story or case that makes the stakes personal.

4. **Map the strongest counterargument.** Identify the single most credible objection a reasonable skeptic would raise. Draft a one-sentence steel-man of that objection.

5. **Write the rebuttal.** Provide a concise rebuttal (2–3 sentences) that acknowledges the counterargument's validity before dismantling or contextualizing it.

6. **Draft the call to action.** Write a specific, actionable closing directive aimed at the target audience — what should they do, believe, or demand after reading?

7. **Flag logical gaps.** Note any claims in the scaffold that need stronger evidence, any logical leaps, or any emotional appeals that could undermine credibility.

## Output Format

Produce a structured scaffold with labeled sections in this order:

```
CENTRAL THESIS
[One sharp, contestable sentence]

TARGET AUDIENCE & TONE
[2–3 sentences: who they are, what they already believe, appropriate register]

SUPPORTING PILLARS
1. Empirical — [claim + specific evidence or data point to source]
2. Logical/Causal — [reasoning chain in 2–3 sentences]
3. Anecdotal/Human — [brief story premise or case study in 2–3 sentences]

COUNTERARGUMENT (Steel-Man)
[1 sentence stating the strongest opposing view fairly]

REBUTTAL
[2–3 sentences acknowledging then dismantling the counterargument]

CALL TO ACTION
[1–2 sentences: specific, directed at the audience, actionable]

GAPS TO ADDRESS
- [Bullet list of missing evidence, weak links, or risky claims]
```

Total output length: 300–500 words. Use plain prose within each section — no nested bullets except in Gaps. Do not write the full op-ed; produce only the scaffold.
