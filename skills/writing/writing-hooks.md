# Writing Hooks

This skill generates powerful opening lines and hooks for articles, blog posts, emails, and social content that compel readers to keep reading.

## Hook Writing Process

1. **Identify the reader's core tension** — Ask what the reader fears, wants, or believes before you write anything. A hook only works if it speaks to something the reader already feels.

2. **Choose a hook type based on context:**
   - **Provocative question** — Challenge an assumption the reader holds ("What if everything you know about productivity is making you less effective?")
   - **Bold claim** — State something counterintuitive or surprising as fact ("Most advice about writing is wrong.")
   - **Vivid scene** — Drop the reader into a specific moment ("It was 2 a.m. when the email arrived that changed how I write forever.")
   - **Statistic with stakes** — Lead with a number that reframes the problem ("83% of readers leave a page within 15 seconds.")
   - **Direct address** — Speak to the reader's exact situation ("If you've ever stared at a blank first line for 20 minutes, this is for you.")
   - **Contrast or paradox** — Juxtapose two unexpected ideas ("The best writers I know are also the worst perfectionists.")

3. **Write 5 variations** — Generate at least five distinct hooks using different types. Quantity forces range and prevents fixating on the first decent idea.

4. **Test each hook against these criteria:**
   - Does it create a question in the reader's mind?
   - Does it promise a payoff worth reading for?
   - Is it specific rather than generic?
   - Would the reader feel it was written for them?

5. **Cut ruthlessly** — Remove any hook that relies on vague words like "interesting," "important," or "amazing." Replace abstractions with concrete details.

6. **Match tone to medium** — A newsletter hook can be personal and conversational. A thought leadership article hook should feel authoritative. A cold email hook must be hyper-relevant to the recipient's world.

## Output Format

Produce exactly this structure:

**Hook Variations (5):**
List each hook numbered 1–5, one per line, no extra commentary between them.

**Recommended Pick:**
Identify which hook is strongest and state in one sentence why it works (what tension it creates or what promise it makes).

**Why It Works:**
Two to three sentences explaining the psychological mechanism — what emotion, curiosity, or self-interest it triggers in the reader.

**Optional Follow-up Line:**
One sentence that could immediately follow the chosen hook to deepen momentum before the body begins.

Total length: under 300 words. No headers beyond the four listed above. No filler phrases like "Great question!" or "Here's what I came up with."
