# Argument Construction

This skill builds rigorous, evidence-based arguments designed to persuade skeptical readers by combining logical structure, anticipatory counterarguments, and credible support.

## Argument Construction Workflow

1. **Clarify the claim**: State the central thesis as a single, falsifiable, specific assertion — not a vague opinion.
2. **Identify the audience**: Determine what the skeptical reader already believes, what objections they will raise, and what evidence they trust.
3. **Build the logical backbone**: Choose a primary argument structure (deductive, inductive, or abductive) and map the reasoning chain from premises to conclusion.
4. **Gather and rank evidence**: List supporting evidence in descending strength — empirical data, expert consensus, case studies, analogies. Label the type and source of each.
5. **Steelman the opposition**: Articulate the strongest version of the counterargument in 1–2 sentences, then directly rebut it using evidence or logic.
6. **Apply rhetorical anchors**: Open with a concrete stakes statement (why this matters), and close with a call to action or a clear implication for the reader.
7. **Check for fallacies**: Scan for straw man, false dichotomy, appeal to authority without substance, and circular reasoning. Remove or fix each.

## Output Format

Produce the argument in this structure:

**Thesis** (1 sentence): The precise claim being argued.

**Stakes** (1–2 sentences): Why the reader should care; what is lost or gained.

**Core Arguments** (numbered list, 2–4 points):
- Each point: claim → evidence → logical link to thesis

**Primary Counterargument + Rebuttal** (2–4 sentences): Strongest objection stated fairly, then dismantled.

**Conclusion** (2–3 sentences): Restate thesis in light of the evidence; end with a specific implication or action.

Length: 300–600 words for standard arguments. Expand only if the topic requires multiple counterarguments or extensive evidence chains.
