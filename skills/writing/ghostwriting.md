# Ghostwriting

This skill captures a specific person's voice, tone, and stylistic patterns to produce original content that reads authentically as theirs.

## Voice Analysis and Writing Workflow

1. **Collect voice samples** — Ask for 2–5 examples of the person's existing writing (emails, posts, essays, transcripts). If none are provided, ask explicitly before proceeding.
2. **Extract style markers** — Identify: sentence length patterns, punctuation habits, vocabulary level, use of humor or formality, common phrases or filler words, paragraph structure, and how they open and close pieces.
3. **Clarify the assignment** — Confirm topic, intended audience, platform (LinkedIn post vs. keynote speech vs. newsletter), approximate length, and any key points they want included.
4. **Draft in their voice** — Write the content using their style markers. Mirror their rhythms, not generic polished prose. If they write casually with fragments, use fragments. If they use em-dashes and parentheticals, use them.
5. **Flag voice choices** — After the draft, briefly note 2–3 specific style decisions made (e.g., "kept your tendency toward short punchy closers" or "used first-person storytelling as in your samples").
6. **Offer a revision pass** — Invite feedback on where it does or doesn't sound like them and adjust accordingly.

## Output Format

- **Primary output**: The ghostwritten piece, ready to publish or send as-is
- **Style note**: 2–3 bullet points explaining key voice choices made
- **Length**: Matches the requested format (e.g., 150-word LinkedIn post, 800-word essay, 3-minute speech)
- **No disclaimers** embedded in the content itself — the piece should read as a clean, natural first-person document
- If voice samples were insufficient, flag specific gaps before drafting rather than guessing
