# Section Length Balance Audit

This skill audits a long-form piece for structural imbalance, flagging sections that are over- or under-developed relative to their role in the document, and delivers specific recommendations to fix each one.

## Audit Workflow

1. **Inventory all sections**: List every heading/section by name and count its approximate word or paragraph length.
2. **Assess each section's role**: Categorize each section as foundational (must be thorough), supporting (moderate depth), or supplementary (brief is fine). Use the document's stated purpose and audience to make this judgment.
3. **Score proportionality**: For each section, compare its actual length against its expected depth based on its role. Flag any section that is more than roughly 2x longer or less than half as long as its role warrants.
4. **Diagnose the cause**: For bloated sections, identify whether the issue is repetition, tangential detail, over-explanation, or padding. For thin sections, identify whether the issue is missing evidence, missing examples, skipped transitions, or underdeveloped argument.
5. **Prioritize fixes**: Rank flagged sections by impact — sections that are central to the document's thesis get highest priority.
6. **Write specific recommendations**: For each flagged section, state exactly what to cut or what to add (not just "expand" or "trim").

## Output Format

Produce a structured audit report with the following sections:

**Section Inventory Table**
| Section Name | Word Count | Role | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| [name] | [count] | foundational/supporting/supplementary | ✅ Balanced / ⚠️ Thin / 🔴 Bloated |

**Flagged Sections**
For each flagged section, provide:
- **Section**: [name]
- **Issue**: [Thin / Bloated] — [one sentence diagnosis]
- **Recommendation**: [2–4 concrete, specific actions — what to add, cut, merge, or move]

**Overall Balance Summary**
- 2–3 sentences describing the document's structural pattern (e.g., front-loaded, weak middle, strong conclusion)
- One priority action the writer should tackle first

Keep the report direct and editorial in tone. Avoid vague advice like "consider expanding." Name the specific content that is missing or redundant.
