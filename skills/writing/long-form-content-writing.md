# Long-Form Content Writing

This skill produces authoritative, well-structured long-form content — guides, essays, and deep-dives — designed to comprehensively cover a topic and establish genuine expertise.

## Workflow

1. **Clarify scope before writing** — Identify the target audience (beginner, intermediate, expert), the desired length range, and the primary goal (educate, persuade, rank, convert). If not provided, state your assumptions explicitly.

2. **Build a strong structural skeleton first** — Before prose, outline H2 and H3 headings that create a logical progression. Long-form content earns authority through structure, not just length. Present the outline to the user if the piece is over 1500 words.

3. **Open with a hook that earns the read** — Lead with a surprising statistic, a counterintuitive claim, a vivid scenario, or a direct statement of stakes. Never open with "In today's world" or generic scene-setting.

4. **Write with depth, not padding** — Every section must add new information, perspective, or evidence. If a point can be made in two sentences, make it in two sentences. Depth comes from specificity: named examples, data points, step-by-step mechanics, and real trade-offs.

5. **Use expert-level vocabulary accurately** — Write at the level of someone who has spent years in the field. Define jargon only when the audience is explicitly general. Avoid over-explaining what the reader already knows.

6. **Embed credibility signals throughout** — Reference real frameworks, methodologies, named researchers, historical context, or documented case studies. Avoid vague appeals like "experts say" or "studies show" without specifics.

7. **Maintain narrative tension** — Long-form content dies when it becomes a flat list. Use problem → complication → resolution arcs within sections. Pose questions, then answer them. Introduce trade-offs, then resolve them.

8. **Close with synthesis, not summary** — The conclusion should deliver a insight or takeaway the reader couldn't have articulated before reading. Avoid restating the intro. End with a clear implication or next action.

9. **Review for authority markers** — Before finalizing, check: (a) every claim is specific, (b) no filler phrases exist, (c) structure guides the reader without hand-holding, (d) the piece could not be confused with AI-generated padding.

## Output Format

- **Length**: Match to scope — 800–1500 words for focused guides, 2000–4000 words for comprehensive deep-dives, 4000+ for definitive pillar content. Never pad to hit a number.
- **Structure**: H1 title, brief deck/intro paragraph, H2 sections with optional H3 subsections, conclusion. Use bullet lists only for genuinely enumerable items — not as a substitute for prose.
- **Tone**: Authoritative but direct. Active voice. No hedging language ("might," "perhaps," "it could be argued") unless expressing genuine uncertainty.
- **Formatting**: Bold key terms or critical takeaways sparingly (maximum 1–2 per section). Use blockquotes for direct quotes or standout insights. Include a TL;DR summary at the top only if the piece exceeds 2500 words.
- **Sourcing**: Where the user has not provided sources, note inline where a citation or data point should be verified/inserted using `[SOURCE NEEDED]` rather than fabricating statistics.
