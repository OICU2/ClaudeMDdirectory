# Long-Form Chapter Transition Builder

This skill drafts polished transition passages between major sections or chapters by synthesizing what was just argued, signaling what comes next, and reinforcing the work's central thesis — Claude activates it whenever a writer needs to bridge two large structural units of a document, essay, or book.

## Workflow

1. **Gather the raw material**: Ask the writer to provide (a) the closing paragraph or main claim of the preceding chapter, (b) the opening paragraph or main claim of the upcoming chapter, and (c) the overarching thesis or central argument of the full work. If any of these are missing, request them before proceeding.

2. **Identify the logical relationship**: Determine how the two chapters connect — is the relationship causal, contrastive, cumulative, complicating, exemplifying, or pivoting? Name it explicitly before drafting so the transition uses the right rhetorical move.

3. **Extract the closing thread**: Pull the key claim, image, or question the previous chapter left unresolved or settled. This becomes the transition's launchpad — do not re-summarize the whole chapter.

4. **Bridge through the thesis**: Write one to three sentences that link the closing thread back to the work's central argument. This reminds the reader why the previous chapter mattered.

5. **Open the forward path**: Write one to three sentences that introduce the upcoming chapter's central problem or claim as the necessary next step. Frame it as arising naturally from what was just established, not as a fresh start.

6. **Avoid these failure modes**:
   - Do not recap the previous chapter at length
   - Do not use mechanical signpost phrases ("In the next chapter, I will…")
   - Do not introduce new evidence or argument inside the transition
   - Do not let the transition exceed 150–250 words unless the writer specifies otherwise

7. **Tune to the register**: Match the prose style (academic, literary, journalistic, conversational) to whatever the writer has provided as sample text. If no sample is given, ask for one sentence describing the intended tone.

## Output Format

Produce a single polished prose passage of 100–250 words. No headers, no bullet points, no labels — just the transition text ready to paste between chapters. After the passage, include a brief bracketed note (2–3 sentences) explaining the rhetorical move made and flagging any assumptions about tone or argument that the writer should verify.
