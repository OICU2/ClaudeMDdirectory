# Thought Leadership Article Development

This skill shapes a subject-matter expert's raw ideas into a publication-ready bylined article with a coherent argument, supporting evidence, and a voice that sounds distinctly like the expert.

## Workflow

### 1. Extract and Clarify the Core Argument
- Identify the single most defensible and non-obvious claim in the notes
- If no clear thesis exists, surface 2–3 candidate arguments and ask the expert to choose
- Reject generic claims ("AI is changing everything") in favor of specific, contestable ones ("Most companies are automating the wrong processes first")

### 2. Audit the Raw Material
- Catalog what exists: data points, anecdotes, frameworks, opinions, examples
- Flag gaps: missing evidence for bold claims, underdeveloped sections, unsupported assertions
- Note any jargon that needs translating for a general professional audience

### 3. Build the Argument Architecture
Structure the article around this spine:
- **Hook**: A counterintuitive insight, provocative question, or concrete scene — not a statistic
- **Stakes**: Why this argument matters now, to this audience
- **Core claim**: One sentence, stated plainly
- **Supporting pillars**: 2–4 distinct reasons, each with evidence or example
- **Counterargument acknowledgment**: Steel-man one objection, then rebut it
- **Actionable takeaway**: What should the reader do or think differently

### 4. Capture and Amplify the Expert's Voice
- Scan the notes for distinctive phrases, recurring metaphors, or characteristic opinions — preserve and amplify these
- Match register to the expert's professional context (e.g., blunt and data-driven for a CFO; conceptual and narrative for a design leader)
- Write in first person unless instructed otherwise
- Avoid corporate filler: "leverage," "synergy," "best-in-class," "move the needle"
- Use short sentences for emphasis. Vary rhythm. Let opinions land without hedging.

### 5. Draft the Full Article
- Write a complete draft without placeholders
- If evidence is missing for a claim, insert a bracketed note: `[EXPERT TO CONFIRM: stat on X]`
- If an anecdote is implied but not told, draft a version with a bracketed note: `[EXPERT TO VERIFY/ADJUST: story as written]`

### 6. Self-Review Before Delivering
- Does every paragraph serve the core argument?
- Does the opening earn attention in the first two sentences?
- Is there at least one moment that will make the target reader think "I've never heard it put that way"?
- Does the ending tell the reader what to do or believe, not just summarize?

## Output Format

Deliver the following in sequence:

**1. Argument Summary (3–5 sentences)**
State the core thesis, the intended audience, the key supporting logic, and the distinguishing angle. This confirms alignment before the expert reads a full draft.

**2. Full Article Draft**
- Length: 600–1,200 words (adjustable for target publication)
- Format: Prose paragraphs with a headline, optional subheadings if the piece is 900+ words
- Headline: Provide 3 options — one declarative, one question-based, one provocative
- No bullet lists inside the article body unless the expert's style explicitly calls for them

**3. Flagged Gaps**
A bulleted list of any claims that need the expert's verification, missing data, or stories that were inferred rather than provided. Each item should specify exactly what is needed and why it matters to the argument.
