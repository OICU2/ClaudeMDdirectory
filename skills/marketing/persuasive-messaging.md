# Persuasive Messaging

This skill applies behavioral psychology, narrative structure, and rhetorical frameworks to craft messages that move a specific audience from passive reading to active response.

## Persuasion Workflow

### 1. Define the Persuasion Target
- Identify the single desired action (click, reply, buy, sign up, share)
- Identify the audience's current belief or emotional state before reading
- Identify the desired belief or emotional state after reading

### 2. Diagnose the Resistance
Determine which friction is blocking action:
- **Awareness gap** — they don't know the problem exists
- **Trust gap** — they don't believe the claim
- **Urgency gap** — they know but don't feel pressure to act now
- **Identity gap** — the action doesn't feel like "something I do"

### 3. Select the Persuasion Framework
Match the framework to the resistance type:

| Resistance | Framework |
|---|---|
| Awareness | Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS) |
| Trust | Before/After/Bridge with social proof |
| Urgency | Scarcity, loss framing, deadline narrative |
| Identity | Aspirational identity + belonging signal |

### 4. Apply Core Psychological Levers
Use at least two of the following where appropriate:
- **Loss aversion** — frame inaction as a cost, not just the absence of gain
- **Social proof** — cite numbers, names, or behaviors of similar people
- **Specificity** — replace vague claims with precise details ("saves 4 hours" not "saves time")
- **Commitment consistency** — reference a small prior agreement before asking for a larger one
- **Narrative arc** — use a protagonist, a conflict, and a resolution the reader can see themselves in

### 5. Write the Message
- Open with the audience's current pain or desire, not your product or solution
- Place the core claim no later than the third sentence
- Use the reader's language, not industry jargon
- End with one clear, low-friction call to action
- Remove every sentence that does not advance the desired belief or action

### 6. Stress-Test the Draft
Ask:
- Would a skeptical version of this audience believe this claim?
- Is there one clear next step, or are there multiple competing asks?
- Does the opening sentence earn the second sentence?
- Is the call to action specific about what happens next ("Start your free trial" vs. "Learn more")?

## Output Format

Deliver the following:

**Persuasion Brief** (3–5 lines)
- Target action
- Audience starting state
- Identified resistance type
- Framework selected

**Message Draft**
- Full message formatted for the intended channel (email, ad, landing page section, social post, script)
- Subject line or headline if applicable
- Body copy with clear paragraph breaks
- Call to action on its own line

**Annotations** (inline or listed)
- Label which psychological lever each key section uses
- Flag any sentence that could be cut without losing persuasive force
- Note one alternative opening if the current hook is high-risk

Length scales to channel: ads = 50–150 words, emails = 150–400 words, landing pages = delivered section by section on request.
