# Marketing Hiring Brief Builder

This skill produces a structured, scoped hiring brief for a marketing role by first diagnosing gaps in channels, capacity, and competencies, then translating those gaps into a precise set of responsibilities and required skills a candidate must cover.

## Diagnostic Intake

Before drafting anything, gather the following from the user. Ask all questions in one message if information is not already provided:

1. **Current team composition** — Who is on the marketing team now, and what does each person own?
2. **Channel coverage** — Which channels are active (SEO, paid, email, social, content, events, PR, partnerships, etc.)? Which are unmanned or underperforming?
3. **Campaign volume** — How many campaigns or projects run per month/quarter? What is falling through the cracks?
4. **Skill deficiencies** — What work is being outsourced, skipped, or done poorly due to missing expertise?
5. **Growth stage and goals** — What are the marketing team's targets for the next 6–12 months?
6. **Seniority and budget range** — Is this an IC role, a lead, or a manager? Is there a comp range in mind?

## Brief Construction Rules

Once inputs are gathered, follow this process:

1. **Map the gap** — List the specific channels, functions, or workloads that are unowned or underserved based on the intake answers. Be explicit: name the channel and the consequence of the gap (e.g., "No one owns SEO — organic traffic is stagnant").

2. **Define the scope** — Determine whether this role is a specialist (owns one channel deeply) or a generalist (covers multiple lighter-touch functions). Do not let the brief be both — pick the one that best addresses the dominant gaps.

3. **Write responsibilities** — Draft 5–7 bullet responsibilities that map directly to the identified gaps. Each bullet must be outcome-oriented (e.g., "Own and grow the email channel from 2 sends/month to weekly cadence, including segmentation and A/B testing").

4. **Write required competencies** — List 5–8 specific, testable skills the candidate must have. Avoid vague terms like "strong communicator." Use concrete equivalents (e.g., "Can write a cold email sequence from scratch without a copywriter").

5. **Flag nice-to-haves** — List 2–4 bonus skills that would expand the role's impact but are not blockers to hiring.

6. **Write a scope boundary** — One short paragraph stating what this role does NOT own, to prevent scope creep during interviews and onboarding.

7. **Suggest an interview signal** — Recommend one practical screen or work sample that would validate the most critical competency (e.g., "Ask candidates to audit our current paid search account and identify three quick wins").

## Output Format

Produce a structured hiring brief with the following sections in this order:

---

**Role Title:** [Suggested title based on scope and seniority]
**Seniority Level:** [IC / Senior IC / Lead / Manager]
**Reports To:** [Inferred or user-provided]

**Why We're Hiring This Role**
2–3 sentences explaining the specific gap this hire fills, grounded in the diagnostic inputs. No generic language.

**Core Responsibilities**
- [5–7 outcome-oriented bullets]

**Required Competencies**
- [5–8 specific, testable skills]

**Nice-to-Have Skills**
- [2–4 bonus skills]

**What This Role Does Not Own**
[1 short paragraph defining scope boundaries]

**Recommended Interview Screen**
[1 practical exercise or signal with rationale]

---

Length: approximately 400–600 words. Use plain language. Avoid corporate jargon. The brief should be us
