# Brand Storytelling

This skill builds cohesive, emotionally resonant brand narratives by uncovering core purpose, defining the brand's role in the customer's journey, and translating identity into language that connects across every touchpoint.

## Brand Narrative Development Process

### 1. Extract the Brand Foundation
Ask or infer the following if not provided:
- What problem does the brand solve, and for whom?
- What is the founding story or origin moment?
- What does the brand believe that competitors do not?
- What transformation does the brand create in a customer's life?

### 2. Identify the Narrative Archetype
Map the brand to one of these core story roles:
- **The Hero's Guide**: Brand as mentor helping customers achieve their goals (e.g., Nike)
- **The Challenger**: Brand disrupting a broken status quo (e.g., Oatly)
- **The Belonging Builder**: Brand creating community and shared identity (e.g., Harley-Davidson)
- **The Visionary**: Brand pulling people toward a better future (e.g., Tesla)
- **The Caretaker**: Brand protecting or nurturing something customers value (e.g., Patagonia)

### 3. Build the Core Narrative Arc
Construct the brand story using this structure:
- **The World Before**: What was broken, missing, or frustrating before this brand existed?
- **The Inciting Truth**: What belief or insight drove the brand into existence?
- **The Brand's Role**: What specific role does the brand play — not the hero, but the enabler?
- **The Transformation**: What does life look like for a customer after engaging with the brand?
- **The Stakes**: What is lost if the brand fails or the customer ignores it?

### 4. Develop the Brand Voice Markers
Define 3–5 specific language characteristics:
- Tone descriptors (e.g., "direct but warm, never corporate")
- Words the brand owns (e.g., "craft," "obsessed," "unapologetic")
- Words the brand never uses (e.g., "solutions," "synergy," "leverage")
- Sentence rhythm (short and punchy vs. considered and expansive)

### 5. Write the Narrative Artifacts
Produce the following based on context:
- **One-line brand truth**: The single sentence that captures what the brand stands for
- **Brand origin story** (150–250 words): Human, specific, emotionally grounded
- **Brand manifesto** (200–350 words): Declarative, belief-driven, written in brand voice
- **Elevator narrative** (50–75 words): For use in pitches, bios, and introductions

### 6. Pressure-Test the Story
Check every narrative element against these filters:
- Could a competitor say this? If yes, rewrite it.
- Does it center the customer's transformation, not the brand's features?
- Is there a specific human truth embedded, not a generic claim?
- Does it make the brand's point of view unmistakably clear?

## Output Format

Deliver a structured Brand Story Document with these clearly labeled sections:

**Brand Foundation Summary**
- Core belief (1 sentence)
- Customer transformation (1 sentence)
- Narrative archetype (name + 1-line rationale)

**Brand Voice Guide**
- Tone: 3–5 descriptors
- Own these words: bulleted list of 4–6 terms
- Never use: bulleted list of 3–5 terms
- Rhythm note: 1 sentence describing sentence style

**Narrative Artifacts**
- One-line brand truth
- Brand origin story (150–250 words, paragraph form)
- Brand manifesto (200–350 words, paragraph or stanza form)
- Elevator narrative (50–75 words)
