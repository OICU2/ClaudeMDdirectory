# Competitive Pricing Perception Map

This skill builds a structured map of how your target audience perceives your pricing relative to competitors and generates specific messaging and positioning adjustments to shift that perception.

## Workflow

### 1. Gather Inputs
Ask the user for the following if not already provided:
- Their product/service name and price point
- 2–5 competitor names and their known price points (or approximate tiers)
- Target audience segment (role, industry, company size)
- Current pricing complaints or objections heard from prospects or customers
- Any existing positioning language they use around pricing (e.g., "premium," "affordable," "best value")

### 2. Map the Perception Landscape
Construct a 2×2 perception matrix with axes:
- **X-axis**: Perceived Price (Low → High)
- **Y-axis**: Perceived Value (Low → High)

Place the user's product and each competitor on the map based on:
- Stated price points
- Market positioning signals (e.g., enterprise vs. SMB focus, feature density, brand tone)
- Any objections or comparisons the user has shared

Label each quadrant:
- Top-right: **Premium / Justified**
- Top-left: **Hidden Gem / Underpriced**
- Bottom-right: **Overpriced / Misaligned**
- Bottom-left: **Budget / Commodity**

Identify which quadrant the user currently occupies vs. where they want to be.

### 3. Diagnose the Perception Gap
Identify the specific mismatch between actual and perceived position:
- Is the price perceived as higher than value delivered? (messaging problem)
- Is the price perceived as lower than value, undermining trust? (positioning problem)
- Are competitors anchoring expectations in a way that disadvantages the user? (framing problem)

Name the gap clearly: e.g., "You are priced mid-market but perceived as budget because your messaging focuses on cost savings rather than outcomes."

### 4. Generate Positioning Adjustments
Produce 3–5 specific messaging or positioning changes:
- Reframe price anchors (e.g., shift from per-seat to per-outcome pricing language)
- Identify proof points that signal value at the stated price tier
- Suggest competitor comparisons to make or avoid in sales conversations
- Recommend which audience signals (case studies, logos, metrics) reinforce the target quadrant
- Flag any product packaging or naming conventions that create perception drag

### 5. Draft Messaging Variants
Write 2–3 short positioning statements (1–2 sentences each) that reflect the target quadrant perception. Each should:
- Address the primary objection surfaced in step 3
- Use language calibrated to the target audience segment
- Avoid price-first framing unless the strategy is cost leadership

## Output Format

Produce the following sections in order:

**Perception Map Summary**
A text-based 2×2 grid showing placement of the user's product and each competitor, with quadrant labels. Use ASCII or markdown table formatting.

**Current vs. Target Position**
Two lines stating: where the product currently sits and where it should be, with a one-sentence explanation of the gap.

**Perception Gap Diagnosis**
3–5 bullet points naming the specific signals, language patterns, or market dynamics causing the misalignment.

**Positioning Adjustments**
Numbered list of 3–5 concrete changes to messaging, framing, or packaging with a one-sentence rationale for each.

**Messaging Variants**
2–3 ready-to-use positioning statements, each labeled by use case (e.g., "For sales calls," "For landing page headline," "For competitive battlecard").

Total output length: 400–700 words. Use headers for each section. No preamble.
