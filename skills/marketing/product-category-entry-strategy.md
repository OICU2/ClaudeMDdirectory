# Product Category Entry Strategy

This skill produces a full go-to-market entry plan when a company or product is moving into a new category, covering positioning, messaging hierarchy, channel priorities, and phased launch sequencing.

## Strategy Development Workflow

### 1. Clarify Entry Context
Before building the plan, establish:
- What product or offering is entering the category
- What category is being entered and why now
- Who the target buyer is (existing customers, net-new, or both)
- Whether this is a greenfield launch, pivot, or expansion play
- Key constraints: budget tier (shoestring / mid / well-funded), timeline, team size

If any of these are missing, ask for them before proceeding.

### 2. Define Category Positioning
- Name the category clearly — avoid ambiguity about what space you're competing in
- Identify the dominant incumbent(s) and the default alternative buyers use today
- Write a positioning statement using this structure: "For [target buyer], [product] is the [category] that [primary differentiator], unlike [incumbent/alternative] which [key weakness]."
- Choose one of three entry angles: challenger (displace incumbents), niche-owner (dominate a segment incumbents ignore), or category creator (reframe what the category is)

### 3. Build Messaging Hierarchy
- **Primary message**: One sentence a buyer remembers after a 30-second conversation
- **Three proof points**: Concrete, verifiable claims that support the primary message
- **Objection responses**: For each of the top 2–3 objections a new entrant will face, write a one-sentence rebuttal
- **Competitive contrast statement**: One sentence explaining why this product now vs. the established option

### 4. Select Channels
Recommend 2–3 primary channels based on the buyer profile and entry angle. For each channel:
- State why it fits this specific category entry (not generic reasoning)
- Identify the content format or tactic that works in this channel for category education
- Flag one risk or failure mode for that channel in this context

Typical channels to evaluate: content/SEO, paid search, community-led, partnerships, outbound sales, analyst/press, product-led growth, events.

### 5. Define Launch Sequencing
Break the launch into three phases:
- **Phase 1 — Validate (weeks 1–6)**: Closed beta or limited release. Goal: confirm ICP, collect 3–5 reference customers, stress-test messaging with real buyers.
- **Phase 2 — Seed (weeks 7–16)**: Expand to first 50–100 customers. Activate 1–2 primary channels. Produce category-level content. Begin analyst/press outreach if applicable.
- **Phase 3 — Scale (week 17+)**: Broaden channel mix, introduce demand generation, operationalize sales motion. Define the metric that triggers moving to this phase.

### 6. Define Success Metrics
For each phase, specify 1–2 leading indicators (e.g., ICP conversion rate, CAC by channel) and 1 lagging indicator (e.g., category share, revenue from new segment).

## Output Format

Produce a structured markdown document with these labeled sections:

1. **Category Entry Summary** — 3–5 bullet brief: product, category, entry angle, target buyer, timeline
2. **Positioning Statement** — formatted using the template from Step 2
3. **Messaging Hierarchy** — primary message, three proof points, objection responses, competitive contrast
4. **Channel Plan** — table with columns: Channel | Rationale | Tactic | Risk
5. **Launch Sequence** — three-phase timeline with goals, activities, and phase-gate metrics
6. **Success Metrics** — table with columns: Phase | Leading Indicator | Lagging Indicator

Length: 600–1,000 words. Use
