# Ideal Week Design Builder for Solopreneurs

This skill creates a personalized, time-blocked weekly template that allocates hours across billable client work, marketing, admin, and deep work so a solopreneur can operate proactively instead of reactively.

## Discovery Questions

Before building the template, gather this information (ask all at once if not already provided):

1. **Work hours**: How many hours per week do you want to work? What days?
2. **Revenue split**: What percentage of your income comes from client work vs. other sources (products, content, courses)?
3. **Current bottleneck**: What consistently falls through the cracks — marketing, admin, client delivery, or personal projects?
4. **Energy map**: When are you sharpest (morning, afternoon, evening)? When do you hit a slump?
5. **Fixed commitments**: Any recurring calls, school pickups, or non-negotiables already on the calendar?
6. **Business stage**: Are you in growth mode (need more clients) or sustainability mode (have enough, need systems)?

## Week Design Rules

Apply these principles when building the template:

**Rule 1 — Protect deep work first.**
Schedule the highest-cognitive-effort work (client delivery, content creation, strategy) during peak energy hours before filling anything else in.

**Rule 2 — Batch by category, not by client.**
Group all admin tasks together, all sales/marketing together, and all client-facing work together. Never alternate between categories within a half-day block.

**Rule 3 — Allocate by revenue priority.**
- Growth mode: 40% billable work, 30% marketing/sales, 20% admin/ops, 10% learning/strategy
- Sustainability mode: 55% billable work, 20% marketing/sales, 15% admin/ops, 10% learning/strategy
- Adjust percentages based on stated goals.

**Rule 4 — Name every block with a purpose, not just a category.**
"Marketing" is too vague. Use "Write 2 newsletter drafts" or "Send 5 outreach messages."

**Rule 5 — Build in buffer blocks.**
Every week needs at least 2 hours of unscheduled buffer for overruns, unexpected client requests, and recovery.

**Rule 6 — Assign theme days when possible.**
Single-theme days (e.g., Monday = client delivery only, Friday = business development only) reduce context-switching and decision fatigue.

## Building the Template

1. Map the total available hours across the chosen workdays.
2. Apply the revenue-priority allocation percentages to calculate hours per category.
3. Slot deep work blocks into peak energy windows first.
4. Assign theme days if the schedule allows (3+ day workweeks).
5. Place admin and communication tasks in low-energy windows (post-lunch, late afternoon).
6. Insert buffer blocks — at least one mid-week, one end-of-week.
7. Flag any category that received zero hours as a gap risk.
