# Service-to-Product Transition Planner

Breaks down a solopreneur's service delivery into discrete steps, identifies which steps are repeatable and systematizable, then maps those steps to viable digital product formats with effort estimates and revenue projections.

## Service Decomposition and Product Mapping Workflow

**Step 1: Extract the full service delivery process**
- Ask the user to walk through every step they take from "client says yes" to "project complete"
- Prompt for: intake/onboarding, discovery/diagnosis, strategy or planning, execution steps, deliverables produced, client communication touchpoints, and offboarding
- If they're vague, ask: "What would a new employee need to do to deliver this service exactly as you do it?"

**Step 2: Classify each step by repeatability and expertise density**
Score each step on two axes:
- **Repeatability**: Is this step nearly identical across all clients? (1 = unique each time, 5 = copy-paste repeatable)
- **Expertise density**: Does this step contain your core IP or judgment? (1 = generic, 5 = highly specialized)

High repeatability + high expertise density = prime productization candidates
High repeatability + low expertise density = automation or template candidates
Low repeatability = keep in custom service offering

**Step 3: Match steps to product formats**
For each high-scoring step, identify the best product format:
- **Templates/frameworks** → steps that produce a structured deliverable (e.g., audit docs, proposals, reports)
- **Courses or workshops** → steps that require client education or mindset shift before execution
- **Toolkits or SOPs** → steps involving a repeatable process the client can run themselves
- **Done-with-you programs** → steps requiring guidance but not full execution
- **Software or tools** → steps involving data processing, calculation, or repeated analysis
- **Community or membership** → steps involving ongoing advice, accountability, or updates

**Step 4: Estimate effort to productize**
For each candidate product, estimate:
- **Build time**: Hours to create the first version (be realistic — add 50% buffer)
- **Validation cost**: Minimum viable version to test with 3–5 buyers before full build
- **Maintenance burden**: How often will this need updating?

Tier the candidates:
- **Quick wins** (under 20 hours to build): templates, checklists, swipe files
- **Medium builds** (20–80 hours): mini-courses, toolkits, group workshops
- **Heavy builds** (80+ hours): full courses, software, certification programs

**Step 5: Estimate revenue potential**
For each product candidate, calculate a realistic revenue range:
- **Price point**: Research comparable products; anchor to value delivered, not time spent
- **Conversion assumption**: Use conservative 1–3% of email list or audience per launch
- **Volume scenario**: Model monthly sales at 5 units, 20 units, and 50 units
- Flag which products have recurring revenue potential vs. one-time purchase

**Step 6: Prioritize the transition roadmap**
Rank products by: (Revenue potential × Repeatability score) ÷ Build effort
Output a sequenced roadmap:
1. One quick-win product to launch within 30 days to validate the model
2. One medium-build product for the 60–90 day window
3. One long-term anchor product to build toward

## Output Format

Produce a structured report with the following sections:

**Service Delivery Map**
A bullet list of every service step identified, each labeled with its Repeatability score (1–5) and Expertise Density score (1–5).

**Productization Candidates Table**
A markdown table with columns: Step | Product Format | Build Tier | Estimated Build Hours |
