# Solopreneur Client Dependency Exit Plan

This skill builds a concrete, phased offboarding or dependency-reduction plan when a solopreneur is over-reliant on one client, guiding them from diagnosis through safe revenue replacement without income collapse.

## Dependency Assessment

Before planning, extract or estimate these numbers:
- **Dependency ratio**: What percentage of monthly revenue comes from this client?
- **Monthly revenue at risk**: Dollar amount if client leaves tomorrow
- **Runway**: How many months of expenses can they cover without that client?
- **Contract status**: Month-to-month, retainer, or project-based?
- **Relationship health**: Is the client unhappy, are they leaving, or is this a proactive move?

Flag severity:
- 50–70% dependency = Elevated risk → 6-month plan
- 71–90% dependency = High risk → 9-month plan
- 91–100% dependency = Critical → 12-month plan with emergency buffer steps

## Phase 1: Stabilize Before You Exit (Months 1–2)

1. **Do not reduce service quality or signal intent to leave** until replacement revenue reaches 30% of the gap.
2. Calculate the **monthly revenue replacement target**: (Dependent revenue) × 0.80 = minimum safe floor before reducing engagement.
3. Identify the solopreneur's **transferable offer**: What service did this client pay for that others would buy?
4. Build or update one public-facing asset (portfolio page, LinkedIn, or landing page) to attract similar clients.
5. Set a **non-negotiable prospecting hour**: Minimum 5 hours/week on outbound or visibility while still serving the dominant client.

## Phase 2: Build the Pipeline in Parallel (Months 2–6)

1. **Target 3 new client prospects per week** using the same ICP (ideal client profile) as the dominant client.
2. Prioritize **quick-win engagements**: smaller scoped projects that convert fast and prove income can come elsewhere.
3. Introduce a **tiered availability policy**: new clients get dedicated slots; dominant client gets slightly reduced (but unannounced) bandwidth.
4. Track a weekly **Dependency Ratio Dashboard**:
   - Current % from dominant client
   - New client revenue this month
   - Pipeline value in proposals sent
5. Set a **trigger threshold**: When dominant client drops below 50% of revenue, move to Phase 3.

## Phase 3: Reduce the Dominant Client Relationship (Months 4–9)

1. **Raise rates or reduce scope** with the dominant client at the next contract renewal or natural checkpoint — frame as a capacity adjustment, not a rejection.
2. Offer to transition them to a **smaller retainer or advisory model** if full offboarding isn't the goal.
3. If full offboarding is needed, give **60–90 days notice minimum** with a written transition summary and optional referral to a replacement freelancer.
4. Create a **client offboarding document** covering: active deliverables, login/access handoff, ongoing support options, and final invoice schedule.
5. Communicate departure professionally: "I'm shifting my practice toward [niche/service] and want to make sure you're set up well before I transition."

## Phase 4: Lock In the New Revenue Baseline (Months 6–12)

1. **Do not replace one dominant client with another.** Set a hard rule: no single client exceeds 30% of monthly revenue going forward.
2. Introduce **retainer contracts** with 2–3 smaller clients to create predictability without concentration.
3. Add one **non-client income stream** (productized service, template, course, licensing) to reduce per-client exposure.
